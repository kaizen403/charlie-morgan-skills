# Outstanding Offer Architecture

> "The offer is the thing that is market-facing, which means that this is what you're going to be using to attract customers. And it's really important. The offer's really the main thing." — Charlie Morgan, *1.6 Outstanding Offer Architecture*

The offer is the highest-leverage component in the business. A great offer with a mediocre pitch beats a mediocre offer with a great pitch. Most struggling consultants are not bad at sales; they have a broken offer and are trying to muscle their way through it.

## The 8-step offer formula

These steps come in this exact order. Source: `week1/16_Outstanding_Offer_Architecture.txt`. The opening directive is: **create as if you are**. Build the offer as if you are already the person who delivers on it; if you don't, you're screwed.

### Step 1 — Niche
Specify exactly *who* this offer is for. Not "businesses." Not "coaches." A precise group with a shared identity, problem set, and price range. (See `niche-selection-grade-a`.)

### Step 2 — Outcome
What specific, measurable result does the niche get? "Grow your business" is not an outcome. "Add 15 qualified sales calls per month into your calendar" is. Outcomes must be:
- **Specific** (a number)
- **Verifiable** (the prospect can tell whether it happened)
- **Aligned with what the niche actually wants** (not what you want to sell)

### Step 3 — Timeframe
By when? "Eventually" doesn't sell. "In the first 30 days" or "within 90 days" or "by end of quarter" sells. The timeframe creates urgency and makes the outcome real.

### Step 4 — Process / steps
What's the rough mechanism? You don't have to give away the whole thing, but the prospect needs to see the *shape* of how you do it. Three to seven named steps. Naming them creates intellectual property.

### Step 5 — Model
How is it delivered? Done-for-you (DFY), done-with-you (DWY), coaching, group, hybrid, software-assisted. The model determines the price ceiling and the kind of client who buys.

### Step 6 — Risk reversal (guarantee)
Charlie:
> "The first thing we can guarantee is that if you don't work with us or make a change to fix this, then you're probably gonna stay stuck with this problem for [period of time]."

Risk reversal removes the buyer's downside. Forms of risk reversal:
- Performance guarantee ("if X doesn't happen by Y, we keep working free until it does")
- Refund guarantee (rare, dangerous, but powerful)
- Milestone-based payments
- "We don't get paid if we don't deliver" structures

A real risk reversal is uncomfortable to make. If you're comfortable making it, it isn't doing the work.

### Step 7 — Summary
The whole offer compressed into one sentence the prospect can repeat to their partner.

> "We help [niche] achieve [outcome] in [timeframe] through [model], guaranteed."

If you can't say it in one breath, the offer isn't done.

### Step 8 — Pricing

> "You wanna work on increasing your price and charging as much as you think you can. You wanna meet this at its peak. Like, you wanna find out what the market's willing to pay and get there as fast as you can."

The constraint on pricing is *your own belief* in what the offer is worth. Increase the actual value, your belief catches up, the market follows. Do not undercharge to feel safe — undercharging filters for bad clients.

## The audit walk-through

When auditing an existing offer, walk through the 8 steps in order and stop at the first that's broken. Don't fix step 5 if step 2 is vague.

| Step | Diagnostic question |
|---|---|
| 1 Niche | Can you name exactly who this is for in one sentence? |
| 2 Outcome | Is the outcome a *number*, not an adjective? |
| 3 Timeframe | Is there a specific deadline? |
| 4 Process | Are there 3–7 named steps? |
| 5 Model | Is the delivery model explicit? |
| 6 Risk reversal | Does the prospect see their downside removed? |
| 7 Summary | Can you state the whole thing in one sentence? |
| 8 Price | Are you pricing at what you believe it's worth, or hiding? |

## Failure modes

- **No specific outcome.** "Grow your business" / "scale to the next level" — vague enough that the prospect can't picture it, can't believe it, can't pay for it.
- **No timeframe.** Without a deadline the brain doesn't engage urgency.
- **No risk reversal.** All risk sits with the buyer. They walk.
- **Pricing from fear.** Charging $1500 because you're scared of $5000. The price is the offer's signal of seriousness.
- **Trying to be everything to everyone.** Add specificity, lose volume, gain conviction.

## Action items

1. Write your offer in one sentence using the summary template: "We help [niche] achieve [outcome] in [timeframe] through [model], guaranteed."
2. If you can't, walk back through steps 1-6 and find the missing piece.
3. Pin the one-sentence summary above your workspace. Read it before every sales call.
