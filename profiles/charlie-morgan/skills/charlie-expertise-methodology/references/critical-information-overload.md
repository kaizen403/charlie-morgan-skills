# Critical Information Overload — Drown Your Brain on Purpose

> "You are like ChatGPT. ChatGPT is trained on a massive corpus of text. You want to treat your methodology build the same way. We just want to get as much information as possible. Literally as much as possible."
> — Charlie Morgan

## When to use this skill

This is **step 2 of the six-step synthesiser process** (after `defining-problem`). Use this skill the moment you can articulate the problem with the W-questions but you don't yet have a methodology. The job here is brute-force input — quantity over quality, no filtering yet, no methodology design. You are an LLM hungry for training data.

Triggers:
- "I need to learn this niche fast"
- "Should I just copy what's already working?"
- "I keep theorising — when do I start consuming research?"
- About to interview an industry expert and don't know how to prep
- Stuck because you "don't know enough" — this is the cure

## The rules of overload

> "Don't spend any more than a few days in information overload. It's easy to use this stage of your startup as an excuse to avoid paying work."

### Rule 1 — Quantity, not quality
Imagine a finite number of media pieces in the universe that contain information on this problem. **Find all of them.** YouTube videos, blog posts, books, interviews, podcasts, Reddit threads, tweet storms, Google Scholar, documentaries, ChatGPT prompts. Do not filter for "is this good?" yet.

### Rule 2 — Total, royal obsession for 3–4 days only
- 12–16 hours per day
- No gym, no socialising, no video games
- Eat / bathroom / sleep — that's the only deviation
- "It's gotta be so bad that you can't fall asleep and you start dreaming about the problem"
- This is short-burst insanity, not a permanent lifestyle

### Rule 3 — Copy your competition shamelessly
> "Be prepared to copy, beg, borrow, steal, and rip off… My entire service delivery, the entire way I solved the problem for my clients was copied directly from my competitors. The ads, the funnels, the nurturing campaigns, the sales training — all copied."

Tactics:
- Book sales calls with competitors as a fake prospect
- Sign up for their lead magnets
- Talk to their clients
- Reverse-engineer their funnels
- Do not invent. Do not be original. Originality is for $1M+ businesses.

This gets you to ~$30–40k/month. To go further you'll innovate — but not yet.

### Rule 4 — Drop pride and authenticity
Pride is the most expensive emotion in business. Anyone who refuses to copy because "I want to be authentic" will fail. The game is the game.

## The three types of information

Every piece of media is a black box containing a mix of:

1. **Critical information** — directly tied to the problem; an actionable lever you (or the client) can pull to cause the desired effect. Rare. The gold.
2. **Standard information** — niche-relevant background, contextual but not directly actionable.
3. **Faulty information** — wrong. Plausible-sounding but factually incorrect. **The most dangerous and most common type.** Often delivered with the highest conviction.

> "Information that's usually the most faulty is information that's delivered with the most conviction."

Example of faulty: "Women retain gym memberships 88% more than men in winter." Sounds smart, might be invented, citations missing. Build your methodology on it and you ruin clients.

(Discernment of which is which happens in `mapping-methodology` — for now, just absorb everything.)

## Source-finding action sequence

### Step 1 — Build the To-Consume List
A messy Google Doc with hyperlinks. Sections:
- Books (every relevant title — Amazon search)
- Podcast episodes
- YouTube videos and series
- Blog posts and articles
- Interviews
- Email newsletters relevant to the niche
- Documentaries
- Google Scholar / academic papers if the problem warrants it
- Reddit / forum threads
- Twitter/X tweet storms

**It should look like a chaotic bomb of hundreds of links.** That's the goal.

### Step 2 — Cover peripheral problems too
> "Peripheral problems are problems that your niche will be facing in addition to, before, after, or because of the problem you're aiming to solve."

If you're a gym retention consultant and you've exhausted retention content, look at: gym acquisition, hiring, cash flow, member experience. Critical info hides in adjacent problem-spaces.

### Step 3 — Key Player Obsession List
Make a list of **everyone** who has solved this problem:
- Recognised experts and thought leaders
- Direct competitors
- Clients of competitors who've gotten results
- Random niche operators who figured it out themselves
- Influencers / podcast hosts in the space

For each person:
- Find every podcast they've appeared on, even 10-view episodes from 3 years ago
- Read everything they've published
- Identify who is *the* GOAT of the space
- Note who has solved it for *themselves* (interview targets — see step 5)

> "If someone's solved this problem, we know where to drill. We are the oil company that knows every single time where to strike oil."

### Step 4 — Consume in obsessive batches
- 12–16 hours/day
- 2x speed where appropriate
- Pause to take notes only on critical-feeling info
- Log: date, source, timestamp/page

### Step 5 — Run interviews **after** the overload, not before
Don't waste experts' time before you're literate. Once you've consumed 100+ pieces of media, you'll know:
- What questions actually matter
- Which "facts" need verification
- What gaps remain

Cold-outreach scripts: "Researching for a book", "PhD survey", "doing apprenticeship", "writing about the industry", "huge fan, 20 minutes please." Beg, prostrate, drop ego. Even one interview can be a goldmine.

## Research resources to maintain

- **Note tracker** — date / source / page / verbatim insight
- **Niche dictionary** — every term you don't fully know, defined
- **Interview note tracker** — separate notes per interview, organised by goal/problem themes
- **Weekly review** — what's emerged as a recurring theme across 5+ sources?

## Panning for gold mental model

> "Imagine you're in a river basin looking for golden nuggets. You're going to have to sift through a ton of dirt, shit, and mess to find pieces of gold."

You will read 100 pages to find 1 sentence worth keeping. That's normal. **Volume creates collisions. Collisions create insight.** A single critical insight uncovered in week 3 can be worth $100k of methodology improvement.

## Do / Don't

**DO**
- Burn 3–4 days hard, then stop
- Add hundreds of links to your to-consume doc
- Copy competitors openly, ethically, ruthlessly
- Talk to clients of competitors
- Capture verbatim quotes with source — never paraphrase the gold
- Treat every speaker as potentially wrong until verified
- Listen at 2x

**DON'T**
- Stay in research forever — it becomes a hiding place
- Filter for quality at this stage (that's step 3)
- Reject info because the source feels uncool
- Try to invent original methodology yet
- Run interviews before doing the bulk consumption
- Trust confident statements without tracing the premise

## Common failure modes

- **Permanent research mode.** Three weeks in, never made a sales call. Antidote: hard 3–4 day cap.
- **Quality-filtering too early.** You miss low-status sources that contain the actual gold.
- **Pride blocking competitor copying.** Costs you 6 months and 5 figures.
- **Believing experts who sound confident.** Always trace back the premise. Charlie's example: someone told him red meat causes cancer; pressed for studies, the person had none.
- **Not interviewing.** Two human conversations beat 50 podcast hours.
- **No notes.** You consume 200 pieces and remember 5%.

## Action items

1. Block 3 to 4 consecutive days in your calendar. Inform anyone who needs to know that you are unreachable.
2. Build a to-consume Google Doc. Target: 100+ links before you start consuming.
3. Build a Key Player list. Target: 15–50 names, ranked.
4. Outreach to 5–10 competitors as a "prospect" this week.
5. Identify 3–5 niche operators who've solved it themselves and reach out for interviews — to be conducted *after* the overload binge.
6. Set up note tracker, dictionary, and interview tracker docs.
