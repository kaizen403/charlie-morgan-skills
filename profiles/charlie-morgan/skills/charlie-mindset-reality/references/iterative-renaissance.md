# Iterative Renaissance

> "Variation is the key of evolution. Once you understand that and you use it, you'll win." — Charlie Morgan, *1.7 Iterative Renaissance*

The Renaissance was an explosion of progress because variation, experiment, and iteration were applied at scale. Charlie reframes business as the same: the operator who runs the most thoughtful experiments wins.

## When to use this skill

- "How do I know if my outreach is working?"
- "Should I change my offer?"
- "I tried something for 2 weeks, didn't work, what's next?"
- "How do I optimize my [funnel/script/copy/cadence]?"
- "I'm scared to change anything."
- "I've been doing the same thing for 6 months and it's plateaued."

## The model

```
Hypothesis → Test → Measure → Decide → Iterate
```

This is the engine of compounding business improvement. Most operators skip steps:

- Skip *hypothesis* → testing without an articulated bet → can't learn from results
- Skip *test* → debating in their head instead of running the experiment
- Skip *measure* → no tracking, no signal
- Skip *decide* → lots of activity, no commitments
- Skip *iterate* → one and done, no compounding

## Charlie's principles for iteration

### 1. Variation is the engine
> "Variation is the key of evolution."

Without variation, no learning. Without learning, no progress. The operator who runs more variations (with measurement) outperforms the operator who runs one.

### 2. Variations must be deliberate, not random
A real test changes one thing at a time. If you change the script, the offer, *and* the cadence in the same week, and results change, you have no idea what caused it. You learned nothing.

> "Change one thing at a time, or change everything and accept you're learning nothing about which lever moved the needle."

### 3. Don't fall in love with version 1
Version 1 exists to be killed by version 2. Treat it as disposable from day one. The point isn't to defend it; the point is to learn what version 2 needs.

### 4. The fastest path forward is *more* iterations, not *better* first attempts
Don't try to perfect before you ship. Ship rough, measure, iterate. The market gives you better feedback than your imagination.

### 5. Measurement before iteration
You cannot iterate on what you don't measure. The bare minimum:

- Outreach: attempts → opens → responses → positive responses → calls booked → calls held → closed
- Sales calls: held → calls that hit each agenda step → calls that get to price → closed
- Offer/copy: variations and their conversion rates side-by-side

A spreadsheet is sufficient. A CRM is better. Zero tracking is fatal.

### 6. The 90-day floor still applies
You can't iterate yourself out of a 5-day test. Below ~30 days, you don't have enough data to make a real iteration decision. Above 90 days of consistent input, you do.

## How a good experiment looks

Bad: "I tried cold email for two weeks. It didn't work."

Good: "I sent 1,200 cold emails over 14 days using script A to a list of 600 prospects in [niche]. Open rate 42%, reply rate 1.8%, positive reply rate 0.3%. I'm hypothesizing that the subject line is the bottleneck because opens are fine but replies are weak. Next variation: same offer/list, new subject lines on the next 600 sends."

The good version has:
- A defined input
- Measured outputs
- An articulated hypothesis about the cause
- A specific change for the next test

## The iteration cycle (concrete)

Walk the user through:

1. **What's the metric you're trying to move?** (One specific number.)
2. **What's the current value?** (If unknown → add tracking before iterating.)
3. **What's the target?**
4. **What's your hypothesis about the bottleneck?** (One sentence.)
5. **What single change tests that hypothesis?** (One change.)
6. **How long do you run the test?** (How many trials are required to be significant?)
7. **What's the decision rule?** ("If reply rate goes up by ≥0.5pp, keep the new subject lines; else revert and test the next hypothesis.")

If the user can't articulate steps 1-7, they're not iterating, they're flailing.

## Common iteration mistakes

- Changing multiple variables simultaneously.
- Iterating on a system that hasn't run long enough to produce signal.
- Iterating without measurement.
- Iterating on output ("I'll close more!") instead of input/cause.
- Falling in love with the original idea and refusing to actually kill it when data says to.
- Quitting iteration on day 5 because the first variation didn't help.

## Action items

1. Pick one metric to move. Write it down.
2. Add tracking if it isn't tracked. (Sheet is fine.)
3. Form a one-sentence hypothesis about the current bottleneck.
4. Define one change that tests that hypothesis.
5. Define your test duration and decision rule before running it.
6. Run. Measure. Decide. Iterate.
