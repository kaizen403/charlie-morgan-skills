# Iterative Expertise

> "Treat your methodology like a pharmaceutical company treats a drug. Version one is never the final version. You're going to be on version 50 in five years. The only way you get there is one variable at a time, with a sample size that's actually big enough to draw a conclusion from." — Charlie Morgan, *3.5 Iterative Expertise*

Expertise compounds. But only if your reps are *deliberate* — one variable changed at a time, measured against a sample big enough to mean anything. Random thrashing after every refund or bad client is the opposite of expertise; it's amateurism dressed up as hustle.

## When to use this skill

- "I just refunded a client. Should I rebuild my whole methodology?"
- "How do I know my service is working?"
- "I've signed 2 clients. One liked it, one didn't. What do I change?"
- "How do real agencies improve over time?"
- "Should I run A/B tests on my delivery?"
- The user is about to make an emotional, sweeping change after a single bad outcome.
- The user has a working methodology and wants to know how to evolve it.

## Why this matters

Methodology is the engine of the business. Sales gets the client; methodology decides whether they stay, refer, and let you charge more next year. Charlie runs an R&D department at EasyGrow specifically because at ~100 new clients/month, the methodology *has* to keep evolving — but the evolution has to be scientific, not panicked.

> "I sign roughly a hundred clients a month into EasyGrow. We have a dedicated R&D department whose entire job is to iterate the methodology. That doesn't mean we change everything every week. It means we change one thing, ship it to the next cohort, measure, and then decide if it stays."

The trap: new operators iterate after every single client. Client 1 hates the onboarding doc → rewrite it. Client 2 hates the kickoff call → restructure it. Client 3 churns → blow up the whole methodology. None of those changes are based on a sample. You're not iterating; you're flailing.

## The four rules of deliberate iteration

### 1. Don't iterate before you have a sample

- 1–2 clients: **no changes.** You don't have data, you have anecdotes.
- 3–5 clients: small, single-variable tweaks only.
- 10–30 clients: meaningful iteration on identified weak points.
- 100+ clients: structural overhauls become possible.

Iterating after client #1 is the entrepreneurial equivalent of switching strategies after one losing hand of poker.

### 2. Ceteris paribus — change ONE variable at a time

> "If you change five things between cohort A and cohort B and it works better, you have no idea which of the five things did it. So next time you'll change all five again, and one of them is probably making it worse, and you'll never know."

Change the kickoff call OR the reporting cadence OR the onboarding doc. Not all three. Run that change against the next batch of clients. Measure. Then change the next thing.

### 3. Follow the scientific method, all four steps

1. **Observation** — what's actually breaking? (Not "I feel like it's broken." Pull the data.)
2. **Hypothesis** — "If I change X, then Y will improve, because Z."
3. **Experiment** — apply the change to the next cohort. Hold everything else constant.
4. **Conclusion** — did the metric move? Keep, kill, or modify.

Skip any step and you're guessing.

### 4. Don't make impulsive emotional changes after refunds

This is the big one. A refund hurts. The instinct is to torch everything and start over so you "feel productive." That instinct is wrong. A single refund is one data point. Wait for a pattern.

> "After a refund, sit on your hands. Do not change the methodology that day. Do not change it that week. Look at three or four refunds in a row and ask: is there a common cause? Then change one thing."

## The execute → measure → adjust loop

This is the heartbeat of iterative expertise.

1. **Execute** — deliver your current methodology, exactly as designed, to the current cohort. No mid-flight tinkering.
2. **Measure** — at the end of the engagement, capture: did they get the result? NPS? Where did friction happen? Did they refer? Did they renew?
3. **Adjust** — pick the single highest-leverage point of failure. Form a hypothesis. Change one variable for the next cohort.
4. Repeat.

Each turn of the loop, your methodology gets sharper. Five years in, you're on version 50, and version 50 outperforms version 1 by 10x. That's the compounding.

## Examples

**Bad iteration:**
- Client churned. Charlie's hypothetical newbie response: rewrites the entire onboarding flow, switches CRM, doubles weekly check-ins, kills the guarantee, adds three new deliverables. Next client also churns. He has no idea why.

**Good iteration:**
- Three of the last five clients all said the same thing on offboarding: "I didn't know what was happening in week 3." Hypothesis: silent week-3 gap is the issue. Change: add a single mid-engagement check-in call in week 3. Hold everything else constant. Run on the next cohort of 5. Compare retention. Keep or kill.

**Charlie's pharma analogy:**
- A drug company doesn't change five molecules in a compound between trials. They change one. They run phase 2 with a sample size big enough to be statistically significant. Then they decide. That's why the drug works. Run your agency the same way.

## Do / Don't

**Do:**
- Treat each cohort of clients as an experiment.
- Document your current methodology as "version X" so you actually know what you changed.
- Pull data, not feelings, when deciding what to change.
- Sit with discomfort after a single bad outcome before reacting.

**Don't:**
- Don't iterate after one client.
- Don't change multiple variables at once.
- Don't rebuild the methodology because you read a tweet.
- Don't panic-spend client money on tools and "fixes" trying to rescue a refund — see `guaranteeing-results` for why.
- Don't confuse activity with iteration. Rewriting your onboarding doc for the seventh time is not progress.

## Action items

1. Write down your current methodology in a single doc. Label it "Methodology v1." Date it.
2. Define the metrics you'll measure on every client: did they get the result, NPS, retention, referral.
3. Commit to a minimum sample size (start at 3) before any change.
4. After each cohort, write a one-page "what changed, what I observed, what I'll change next" note. This is your R&D log.
5. When something goes wrong, before you change anything, ask: "Is this a sample-of-one, or a pattern?"
