# Oscillatory Life Cycles

> "You don't rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your floor. The floor is the average of who you are. To raise the floor is to raise the life."

## When to use

Load this skill any time the work involves:
- Coaching inconsistency in execution, income, energy, mood, or output
- Diagnosing why someone "can't seem to break through" a revenue ceiling
- Designing the work to *raise the floor* rather than *chase the ceiling*
- Explaining why willpower-based motivation always reverts to the mean
- Predicting when an oscillation will break (one ceiling-break followed by a new floor)

This skill is the operational consequence of `subjugating-reality` (radical responsibility) — once the responsibility is internalized, *what* do you actually do? Answer: change the cycle.

---

## The HVAC analogy

A HVAC unit set to 18°C does not produce a constant 18°. It produces:
- Cool air when the room hits 19°
- Hot air when the room hits 17°
- Continuous oscillation between two extremes around the average target

You are the same. Your life oscillates between two points — a ceiling and a floor — and the average is what you call your "normal life."

Examples of personal oscillations:
- Confidence (some weeks unstoppable, other weeks paralyzed)
- Energy (high-output sprints then collapse)
- Income (10K month, 3K month, 8K month, 2K month)
- Health (gym for 6 weeks then 6 weeks of pizza)
- Discipline (waking at 5am for a month then sleeping till noon)
- Mood (grandiose then depressed)

The oscillation is normal and biological. The pattern of *where* you oscillate is engineered — by your beliefs, identity, and habits.

## The math of oscillation

Let:
- **Ceiling** = the best version of you in a given dimension (highest-revenue month, longest workout streak, most disciplined week)
- **Floor** = the worst version (lowest-revenue month, sloppiest week, total collapse)
- **Average** = (ceiling + floor) / 2

Most people obsess over the ceiling. They want to *break* the ceiling — to have one record month, one perfect quarter. This is a strategic mistake.

The leverage point is the floor.

**You don't rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your floor.**

If your worst month is $3K, your average is somewhere around $6–7K, and your ceiling is $10K. You can spike to $15K once. But you'll fall back. Why? Because your *floor* is still $3K — your default behaviors when you're tired, sick, distracted, or in pain produce $3K outcomes. The default catches up.

To produce more income on average, raise the floor. Make the worst version of you produce $7K instead of $3K. Then the average becomes $10K, and the ceiling pulls up to $15K naturally.

## How floors get raised — the breakthrough mechanism

Charlie's actual income progression:

> "I'd oscillate between 10K and 3K on average. Then one month I broke through and did 30K. After that I never went below 10K. New cycle: 30K and 10K. Then I broke 30K — did 50K. New cycle: 50K and 30K. Then 50K became my new floor."

The pattern:

1. You exist in cycle A (e.g., $3K–$10K).
2. One outlier event spikes you above the ceiling. You hit a one-off month at $30K.
3. Your *self-concept* updates. "I'm someone who can do $30K." (Or doesn't update — and you snap back.)
4. If the self-concept updated, the old ceiling becomes the new floor. Cycle B.
5. You oscillate in cycle B until the next breakthrough.

The mechanism is:
- A new performance happens (the spike).
- An identity reorganization happens around it (the self-concept update).
- The new identity pulls all behavior up. The new floor is born.

Without the identity update, the spike is just a fluke. You go right back to the old cycle. This is why "I had one $50K month and then nothing" is so common — the spike happened, but the identity didn't reorganize.

## Why willpower fails

People try to escape oscillation with willpower: "I'll just be more disciplined." This produces:
- A sprint of unusual effort
- A predictable collapse when the effort costs more than the willpower budget
- Return to the average

Willpower is finite. Identity is structural. Change the structure (identity, environment, system) and the floor rises without willpower being the engine.

This is why `unrelenting-discipline` is paired with this skill — discipline is the daily mechanism, but identity (`identity-ignition`) is the structural shift, and `oscillatory-life-cycles` explains why.

## Diagnostic questions

To diagnose where someone is in a cycle:

1. **What's your ceiling in this dimension?** (Best month, best week, peak state.)
2. **What's your floor?** (Worst month, worst week, collapsed state.)
3. **What does your floor look like behaviorally?** (When you collapse, what specifically do you do? Skip outreach? Eat junk? Sleep all day?)
4. **What is your identity's belief about the floor?** ("I deserve a break." "I'm just like that." "Nobody can be on all the time.") — these beliefs *protect* the floor.
5. **What identity update would have to happen for the floor to rise?** ("I'm someone who does 100 outreaches even on bad days.")

The work is to change #5 and let #1 and #2 follow.

## Practical floor-raising tactics

- **Define non-negotiable minimums.** Daily outreach minimum, daily revenue activity minimum, weekly check-in. The floor is what you do *on a bad day*.
- **Engineer the environment.** Remove the path of least resistance to the floor behavior. Block apps. Hide the snacks. Schedule the gym slot.
- **Stack identities.** "I am someone who does X" repeated until the identity self-corrects when X is missed.
- **Use spikes as identity evidence.** When a $30K month happens, do not let the identity stay "I'm a $10K guy who got lucky." Force the update: "I'm a $30K guy now."
- **Attack the floor's protective beliefs.** "I deserve a break" / "I'm just stressed" / "It's been a hard week" — these are the language of the old floor. Notice them, name them, refuse them.

---

## Pitfalls

- **Chasing one big month.** It feels like progress. Without identity update, it's a temporary spike.
- **Comparing your ceiling to someone else's average.** ("They make $50K every month. I had one $50K month. I'm close.") No — their floor is $50K, your floor is $5K.
- **Treating the floor as fixed.** "This is just how I am." This belief *is* the floor.
- **Celebrating the breakthrough and resting.** The breakthrough is when the work begins, not ends. The new identity has to harden.
- **Conflating average with ceiling.** Most people quote their ceiling when asked about income. Reality is the average.
