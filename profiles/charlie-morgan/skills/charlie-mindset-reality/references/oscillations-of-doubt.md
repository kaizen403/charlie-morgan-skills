Triggered when the user is on a losing streak, asks "should I change my script?", is panicking about close rate after a few bad calls, asks "is something wrong with my offer?", is oscillating between confidence and doubt, considers quitting, or is making emotion-driven decisions about their business.

# Oscillations of Doubt

> "Most salespeople fail because they just lose control of their emotions. You can have the best script in the world, you can have the best training… but if you can't keep your own mind in check, then nothing's gonna work for you." — Charlie Morgan, *4.7 Oscillations of Doubt*

Sales is a noisy signal. Your close rate is an average that only reveals itself across many calls. In between, you'll experience peaks (winning streaks) and troughs (losing streaks) — and most salespeople blow up their business *during the troughs* by changing things that don't need changing. This skill is about staying rational across the oscillation.

## When to use this skill

- "I just lost 4 calls in a row, what's wrong?"
- "Should I change my script / offer / niche?"
- "I think my pitch isn't working anymore."
- "I'm in a slump."
- "I'm panicking about my close rate."
- "I just had 3 wins in a row, am I a god now?" (yes, this also.)
- "I want to quit."
- The user is about to make an emotion-driven business change.

## The mental model: mean and trend

```
                          *
                    *           *
mean ─────────*─────────*───────*──── (the average)
         *                   *
   *                              *
        ↑ trough         ↑ trough
                ↑ peak

(the trend = the noisy zigzag oscillating around the mean)
```

> "Most salespeople are sucked into the strong emotional influence that the black line has, which really is just simply the oscillation around the average. Numbers aren't tidy, they're not neat. It takes some time and a big enough data set for them to actually show us the average."

You have an average close rate. It only reveals itself across enough data. Within that data, your actual sequence will be: lose-lose-win-lose-win-win-win-lose-lose-lose-win-… The troughs feel terminal. They are not terminal. They are part of the average.

Charlie's example: he didn't panic during a 10-call losing streak — but most agency owners would panic by call 4. The panic is what kills them.

## The two oscillation phases

### Phase A: The Trough (losing streak)

Symptoms:
- 3+ losses in a row.
- Internal monologue spiraling: "the script is broken," "my niche is wrong," "the offer doesn't work."
- Itch to change things.
- Loss of confidence in the pitch.
- Avoiding looking at the calendar.

What's actually happening: you've hit a normal cluster of losses on the way to the average. Nothing is broken. The trough *feels* terminal because you can't see the peak that's coming.

### Phase B: The Peak (winning streak)

Symptoms:
- 3+ closes in a row.
- Inflated certainty: "I've cracked it, I'm a god."
- Tendency to coast, get sloppy, skip prep.
- Belief the script no longer needs work.

This is also dangerous. Charlie spent a year stuck at 30% because the streak made him stop iterating (see `script-modification`).

### Phase C: Anchored to the mean (where you want to live)

Symptoms:
- You log calls neutrally.
- Wins don't make you cocky. Losses don't make you spiral.
- You're running the 30-call rule.
- You're observing the trend, not living inside it.

## Identifying which phase you're in

A practical diagnostic:

| Signal | Trough | Anchored | Peak |
|---|---|---|---|
| Last 3 calls | 0–1 wins | mixed | 2–3 wins |
| Internal voice | "something's wrong" | "let's see what 30 calls says" | "I've cracked it" |
| Itch to change script | high | none | low (complacent) |
| Looking at recordings | avoiding | regularly | not bothering |
| Emotional tone | dread | flat | euphoric |

If you're in Trough or Peak, you should not be making business decisions. You should be running calls.

## The 30-call rule (the anchor)

> "You must not make any adjustments or iterations to your script, your pitch, your offer, your guarantee, or your business until you've done 30 full calls."

> "Most agency owners, they do four calls, they get four nos, and then they go back to the drawing board in an emotional fit of panic… The average takes some time to show, and the thing that prevents us from allowing that time to happen is emotion."

The 30-call rule is the single mechanism that breaks the oscillation. Until you've done 30 calls on a given version of your script, you do not have data. You have noise. Treat it as noise.

> "The biggest cause of extinction among salespeople: they simply don't try, or don't try for a long enough period of time."

If your brain is screaming to change things at call 12, that's the bedrock failure mode. Note the urge. Don't act on it. Run to call 30.

## The doubt cycle (and how it manifests beliefs)

When you sit in a trough without a stable anchor, the brain manufactures explanations. Those explanations crystallize into beliefs. Those beliefs then drive behavior. Those behaviors create self-fulfilling prophecies.

> "Don't let your negative emotions manifest into shitty beliefs that aren't an accurate depiction of reality only for them to spiral out of control and ruin your agency. If your agency isn't growing, it's probably just because you've got one or two beliefs in your mind that are holding you back."

The classic spiral:

```
3 losses in a row
   ↓
"my niche is too saturated"
   ↓
follow more competitors on social
   ↓
see only competitor content all day
   ↓
belief reinforced: "yeah, way too saturated"
   ↓
half-ass next 5 calls because "what's the point"
   ↓
lose those too
   ↓
quit niche / change everything / blow it up
```

The fix is at the top of the spiral: catch the belief as it forms.

## Anchoring techniques

### 1. The 30-call commitment (structural anchor)

Already covered. Pre-commit. Write it down. The commitment exists *before* the trough so the trough can't talk you out of it.

### 2. Quote Carl Jung at yourself

> "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will rule your life, and you will call it fate."

Most emotional damage in sales is unconscious. Make it conscious by naming it.

### 3. Noting (after every call)

> "Note your emotions in the sales tracker after every call while they're still hot. Just noting them will weaken them."

After every call, log: outcome (win/loss), and the emotion you felt during/after. The act of writing it disarms it.

> "Most of emotional management is just knowing there's something to manage in the first place."

### 4. Daily 20-minute meditation

> "Every day, 20 minutes, sit down, focus on the breath, do nothing else. Whenever you get distracted, back to the breath. I don't care if you have to bring yourself back to the breath 16,000 times in one session. The action of doing it consistently will transcend that, and you will eventually start to notice yourself doing that without realizing it when you are in a sales situation or when you feel emotional."

The mechanism: meditation trains the meta-cognitive muscle that lets you observe the oscillation rather than be inside it.

### 5. Journal during slumps

When a negative emotion rises, write what triggered it, what belief it implies, and whether the belief is actually true. The act of questioning it on paper usually dissolves it.

### 6. Look at the longer dataset

Pull up your numbers from the last 60–90 days. The trough you're in right now is probably one of several you've already survived. Pattern-match it to historical data, not to the panic of the moment.

## Charlie's own data

Charlie shared his own February 2020 data: 24 calls, 10 closes (~45%). The actual sequence was something like:

> "We start off by losing three… then we have one win, then we lose two, then we win, then we lose, then we win two, then we lose two, then we win one, then we lose three, then we win three, then we lose two."

A 45% close rate month *contained* a 3-loss streak and another 3-loss streak. If he'd panicked at any of those, he'd have torched a winning month.

> "If you observe this oscillation, it goes up and then it goes down, then it goes up, then it goes down. But really, it's wrapping itself around the average. As long as the average is fine, then we're fine."

## Doing during the trough

When you're in a trough, the prescription is the opposite of what your brain wants:

| Brain wants | What to actually do |
|---|---|
| Change the script | Run 1 more call on the same script |
| Change the niche | Run 1 more call on the same niche |
| Lower the price | Hold the price |
| "Take a break to think" | Book the next call |
| Read a new sales book | Listen back to your last 3 calls |
| Quit | Note the urge. Run the next call. |

Quantity is the antidote to the trough. The only way to lose is to give up.

> "Quantity will pull you out of a rut, and it's the only way to win, because the only way to lose is to give up."

## Doing during the peak

Equally important. When the streak hits:

- Don't get cocky.
- Don't skip prep.
- Don't decide your script is finalized.
- Keep listening back to recordings.
- Note that you're in a peak so you don't mistake it for the new mean.

The peak is the most dangerous place to stop iterating. (Charlie's 30% plateau for a year.)

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Run the 30-call rule. Always. No exceptions.
- Note emotions after every call.
- Meditate daily. Yes, actually.
- Identify which phase (trough / anchored / peak) you're in before making any decisions.
- Treat clusters of losses as expected, not as evidence.
- Pull up your longer-window data when panicking.

**Don't**
- Don't change your script, offer, niche, or guarantee mid-streak.
- Don't make business decisions while emotional.
- Don't blame "unmotivated prospects" — that's the spiral talking.
- Don't quit during a trough. The peak is right behind it.
- Don't get arrogant during a peak. The trough is right behind it.
- Don't follow competitors compulsively — it manufactures the "saturation" belief.

## Action items

1. Pre-commit to the 30-call rule in writing today. Pin it where you make sales decisions.
2. Set up a sales tracker with a column for outcome AND a column for post-call emotion.
3. Schedule a daily 20-minute meditation. Block the calendar.
4. Pull your last 30–90 days of calls. Map the actual oscillation pattern. Notice you've already survived multiple troughs.
5. Identify the 1–2 beliefs your brain produces during a trough ("my niche is saturated", "the offer doesn't work"). Write them down. They are the enemy.
6. Next time you feel the itch to change something mid-streak — don't. Run the next call instead.
