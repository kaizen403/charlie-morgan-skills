# Defining the Problem — Diagnose Before You Prescribe

> "Prescription before diagnosis is malpractice. If you don't define the problem properly — i.e. you don't understand the problem properly — then any solution you create is going to be mismatched, disjointed, and it's not gonna work."
> — Charlie Morgan

## When to use this skill

This is **step 1 of the six-step synthesiser process** (see `critical-synthesising`). Use this skill the moment you catch yourself jumping to "what should I sell / what's the offer / what funnel / what software" before you can articulate, in cold and ruthless detail, *what the actual problem is, who has it, why it exists, and what it costs them.*

Specifically use it when:
- You feel a vague urge to "just build something"
- A client describes a symptom and you want to immediately fix it
- You're packaging an offer but it feels generic
- Sales calls keep going nowhere — likely you're solving the wrong problem
- You're picking critical information but don't know what's "critical" against
- Multiple competitors solve "the same" problem yet only some succeed — you need to find the real problem under the apparent one

## First principles of a problem

A problem only exists when **all four** of these are true:

1. **A current state** (e.g. broke, fat, no leads, churning members)
2. **A desired state** (rich, fit, full pipeline, retained members)
3. **A gap between them**
4. **A human who perceives it and cares enough to change**

> "Problems don't exist without desires or goals. The desire, need, or goal gives birth to the problem."

Two — and only two — root causes for any unsolved problem:
- **Lack of information** (don't know how) — the consultant's domain
- **Lack of resources / time / constraints** (can't act on what they know)

> "If the problem is big enough, then the person will find the resources, find the time, or alleviate the constraints. It's usually a lack of information, and that's what we're focused on."

The Stoic frame: **the obstacle is the way.** The problem is not your client's enemy. It is the universe screaming the exact stepping stone they must step on. Your job is to make that stone walkable.

## The five critical questions: Who / What / Where / When / Why

> "It's impossible to know how to solve the problem if you don't know who's facing it, what it actually is, where it exists and where it happens, when it happens, and why it happens."

Run this against your niche before writing a single line of methodology.

### WHO experiences the problem
- Demographics, psychographics, level of niche maturity
- What must already be true about them to *have* this problem? (e.g. "running ads → must have a proven offer + cash + history")
- What stepping stones have they already stepped over to reach this one?
- At what point on the niche's milestone chart does your solution become relevant?

### WHAT actually is the problem
- Not the symptom — the *root cause*
- Symptoms in personal life (stress, sleep, relationships, lifespan)
- Symptoms in professional life (revenue, hires, churn, hours)
- What environmental factors influence it?
- What have they already tried? What do they hate about solving it?
- What belief systems do they hold about the problem?
- What is the **true nexus** they're struggling with?

### WHERE does the problem show up
- **Primary places** — obvious manifestation
- **Secondary places** — subtle, second-order locations
- Where do they go to try to solve it? (forums, gurus, software, agencies)
- Where does the niche-as-a-whole stand on solving it?
- Where do they end up if it *isn't* solved?
- Where do they end up if it *is* solved?

### WHEN
- What problems have they faced and solved to *get* to this one?
- What problems will they face *after* this one (load `second-order-problems`)
- How urgent? When does pain become unbearable enough to buy?
- When are symptoms worst? (seasonality, lifecycle stage)
- How long have they had this problem?

### WHY (the most important — by a country mile)
> "Why is so important. You can chuck the rest of it away, really. If you just understand why, then you can get to the how."

Apply **Socratic reasoning**: ask "why" relentlessly until you hit a first principle that cannot be questioned further.

```
Problem: Young guys are broke
  Why? → Not educated on wealth, no discipline, impulsive, lazy, limiting beliefs from parents, broke peers
    Why? → Teachers are broke, society softens men, neuroscience of adolescence, no purpose, intergenerational beliefs
      Why? → ...keep going until you hit bedrock
```

Cause → effect. The problem is the effect. You cannot change effects directly — you change the cause and the effect changes by itself.

## Diagnose, don't solve — the medical model

> "Don't take a solution-based lens yet. Just seek to listen to the problem and find its truth."

Charlie's metaphor: a female friend in tears about her boyfriend doesn't need your fix. She needs you to *understand* before you ever propose action. Same with a client.

A bad doctor misdiagnoses. A *terrible* doctor treats only the symptom.

> "If you have stomach cancer and the doctor just gives you a pill for stomach pain, the symptoms go away — and so does the warning signal. The cancer kills you anyway."

In consulting:
- "I haven't got any leads" is a **symptom**, not a problem
- "My business isn't growing" is a **symptom**
- "I can't keep members" is a **symptom**

The real problem sits one or two layers below. Surface-level symptom-fixing is what cheap consultants do. It's why Charlie spent half a year building mindset modules — *because the real problem isn't lack of sales scripts, it's identity collapse and bad thinking.*

## Critical thinking discipline while diagnosing

While answering the W-questions:
- **Remove yourself.** Don't filter the problem through your trauma or biases.
- **Remove identities.** No emotionally charged collective nouns ("politicians", "carnivores").
- **Identify objects** (MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive).
- **Identify relationships** between objects, like a detective with red string.
- **Identify the time sequence.** What happened in what order.
- **Remove ambiguity.** One word per object. Don't let "shirt / T-shirt / item of clothing" become three objects.
- **Remove emotion.** Just for now.
- **Remove loops.** Don't give a fact more credence because it was repeated.

(See `mapping-methodology` for the full critical-thinking process applied to information.)

## Do / Don't

**DO**
- Spend painful hours on the W-questions before any methodology design
- Ask "why" five+ times for every claimed cause
- Multiple causes per symptom — almost always
- Save answers in Google Drive — you'll revisit constantly
- Re-do this exercise after every batch of 3–5 client sales calls — your understanding will deepen

**DON'T**
- Solve, prescribe, or design while in this phase
- Settle for one-word answers — write paragraphs
- Trust the client's stated problem at face value (they confuse symptom with cause too)
- Fix symptoms that "feel obvious"
- Use this phase to procrastinate on outreach forever — a few solid days of structured diagnosis is enough to start; refine continuously

## Diagnostic checklist before moving to step 2

Before loading `critical-information-overload`, you must be able to answer:

- [ ] Who, in one paragraph, with specific niche-maturity markers
- [ ] What the problem is in plain language *and* what it is NOT (symptoms ruled out)
- [ ] Where it manifests primary + secondary
- [ ] Timeline: prior problems solved, future problems unlocked, urgency
- [ ] At least **5 layers deep** on Socratic "why?"
- [ ] At least **2–4 root causes** identified (problems are usually multifactorial)
- [ ] What information your niche is *missing* to solve it themselves

If any answer is fuzzy, you are not done. Go deeper.

## Common failure modes

- **Solution-mode contamination.** Pretending to diagnose while sneaking in solution thoughts.
- **Stopping at the first "why".** Real causes are 5+ layers down.
- **Single-cause thinking.** Real-world problems have 3–6 entangled causes.
- **Treating the loudest symptom as the problem** because it's where the emotion is.
- **Confirmation bias** — only seeing causes that match the solution you already secretly want to sell.
- **Avoiding the painful ones** — sometimes the root cause is "your niche is delusional / lazy / addicted to a bad belief," and you flinch away. Don't.
