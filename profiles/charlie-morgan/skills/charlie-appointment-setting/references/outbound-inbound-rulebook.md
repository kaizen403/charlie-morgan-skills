# Outbound & Inbound Rulebook

> "The most important thing above everything is consistency. Because it doesn't matter about all the other rules… if you're not consistent, none of it matters." — Charlie Morgan, *5.2 Outbound & Inbound Rulebook*

Every appointment-booking system is one of two things: *outbound* (you reach out to them) or *inbound* (they come to you). Both work. Both have non-negotiable rules. Most beginners break those rules and conclude the channel doesn't work. This skill is the rulebook so you don't.

## When to use this skill

- "Should I do outbound or inbound first?"
- "Cold email vs LinkedIn vs cold calling vs content — which one?"
- "Is outbound dead?" / "Is cold email saturated?"
- "How long does inbound take?"
- "Should I run ads right now?"
- "I'm doing both and neither is working."
- Any time the user is *picking a channel* or *layering multiple channels*.

For the actual machinery of each, load `building-outbound-systems` and `building-inbound-systems`. For the persuasion that makes any channel work, load `asymmetric-psychological-leverage`.

## The core distinction

**Outbound** = you initiate contact with a stranger who is not currently looking for you. You expose them to a stimulus (email, DM, call, ad-to-DM) designed to provoke a reply and a booking.

**Inbound** = you create a stimulus that lives in the world (content, a lead magnet, a YouTube video, a funnel) and the prospect finds it because they're already searching for what you offer. They self-select onto the call.

The two have opposite economics and opposite timelines:

|  | Outbound | Inbound |
|---|---|---|
| Time to first appointment | Days to weeks | 3–12 months |
| Cost to start | $0–$200/month tooling | Time + sometimes ads |
| Quality of prospect | Mid (you chose them) | Higher (they chose you) |
| Quantity ceiling | Capped by labour/tooling | Compounding, uncapped |
| Failure mode | Volume too low or stimulus weak | Premature, no audience |
| Mental load | Daily grind, rejection-heavy | Slow burn, no validation |

## When to do which

**Charlie's strong default for new agency owners: outbound first.** Inbound second, in parallel, as a long-term investment. Paid ads last, after either of the above is profitable.

Why outbound first:

- Faster feedback loop on offer + script + niche.
- Doesn't require an audience.
- Doesn't require capital.
- Forces you to talk to your niche daily, which builds expertise (`niche-immersion-five-steps`).
- The cash flow it produces funds inbound and ads.

Inbound becomes the right primary focus when:

- You have 5+ paying clients and stable cash flow.
- You can dedicate 10+ hours a week to content for 6+ months without expecting return.
- You have something genuinely interesting to say (case studies, frameworks, results).
- You want to scale beyond what manual outbound can produce.

Both at once is fine *only* if outbound is non-negotiable in your week. Most beginners use "I'm doing inbound" as a sophisticated way to avoid the discomfort of outbound. Don't.

## The Outbound Rulebook

### Rule 0: Consistency is everything

> "The only thing — or the most important thing above everything — is consistency. Because it doesn't matter about all the other rules… if you're not consistent, none of it matters."

Outbound rewards the boring person who sends 150 messages every working day for 90 days, not the genius who sends 800 in a sprint and then disappears for two weeks. Build a daily rhythm and defend it like your life depends on it. Because financially, it does.

### Rule 1: Pick one channel, master it, then add another

The temptation when something isn't working is to add a second channel. The right move is almost always to *fix the first one*. Charlie's order of operations:

1. Pick a channel that fits your niche (where they actually live and read).
2. Run it at volume for at least 30 days.
3. Iterate the stimulus weekly.
4. Only after it's producing qualified appointments, add a second channel.

### Rule 2: Volume before optimization

Optimization without data is fantasy. You cannot improve a stimulus you've sent 12 times. You can improve one you've sent 2,000 times. Hit volume first; tweak second.

### Rule 3: The stimulus must be original — but originality alone is not enough

A stimulus that looks like everyone else's gets filed under "another agency, delete." But originality for its own sake (a banana picture, a haiku) doesn't work either. Original *plus* relevant *plus* psychologically loaded (see `asymmetric-psychological-leverage`).

### Rule 4: Follow up more than feels comfortable

Most replies come on follow-ups 4 through 8, not the initial message. Familiarity bias is real. Most beginners stop at follow-up 2.

> "Most of your cold email replies will come from follow-up six plus, because at that point people have typically been exposed to you enough to feel familiar."

Never label your follow-up "this is my last email." Charlie: "You want people to be like, *fuck's sake, how many more times is this guy going to email me?*"

### Rule 5: Track everything

If you cannot tell me your reply rate, your booking rate, your show-up rate, and your close rate from this week's outreach — you are not running outbound, you are doing activity. Numbers or it didn't happen.

### Rule 6: Don't change the script every day

Pick a stimulus. Run it for 100–200 attempts at minimum. Review. Change one variable. Run it for another 100–200. This is the scientific method (`iterative-renaissance`). Daily script changes are panic dressed up as iteration.

### Rule 7: Outbound is a system, not a hustle

The goal from day one is to build something repeatable, documentable, and eventually delegable — not to wing it. Treat every action like you're going to hand it to a VA in 6 months. Because you are.

### Rule 8: The offer has to be good

> "If you can't get clients right now and you're struggling to book appointments… the chances are that it's your foundations that are the problem, it's your mindset that's the problem, it's your expertise or your service that's the problem, or it's your sales process that's the problem."

No outbound system saves a weak offer in a wrong niche. If you're 500 messages in with a 0% reply rate, the diagnosis is upstream — not the script. Reload `niche-selection-grade-a`, `offer-architecture-eight-steps`.

## The Inbound Rulebook

Inbound has fewer rules, but the rules are harder.

### Rule 1: It takes 6–12 months

Don't start inbound expecting cash flow this quarter. If you need cash flow this quarter, do outbound. Inbound is an investment compounding for the version of your business that exists in 12 months.

### Rule 2: Pick one platform, post consistently, for at least 12 months

Same logic as outbound channel selection. Don't try YouTube + LinkedIn + Twitter + TikTok at once. Pick the one your niche lives on and where your strengths sit (long-form thinking → YouTube/LinkedIn; visual/short attention → TikTok/Reels; written precision → Twitter/LinkedIn).

### Rule 3: Every piece of content must have a CTA to the funnel

> "Always, always, always have a call to action to the funnel, and only mention it at the end or if it's really relevant throughout the video."

Content without a CTA is hobby content. The CTA is what turns viewers into appointments.

### Rule 4: Content quality over quantity, but quantity over perfectionism

You are competing for attention with professionals. Mediocre content posted daily for 12 months beats perfect content posted twice. But trash content posted daily forever still loses. Aim for *clearly useful, watchable, every week.*

### Rule 5: The funnel matters as much as the content

Content gets attention. The funnel converts attention into a booked call. A great YouTube channel pointing to a broken funnel is wasted. Build the funnel before you build the audience (see `building-inbound-systems`).

### Rule 6: Speak to your niche, not "everyone"

Generic content gets generic results. Niche-specific content (e.g., "client acquisition for plumbers") gets booked calls from plumbers. Resist the urge to broaden.

### Rule 7: Show, don't tell

Inbound proof beats inbound claims. Show your client results, your calendar, your behind-the-scenes, your process. The show-and-tell bias dominates inbound (see `asymmetric-psychological-leverage`).

## Common mistakes (both)

- **The shiny-object hop.** Three weeks of cold email, "doesn't work," switch to LinkedIn, three weeks, "doesn't work," switch to YouTube, three weeks, give up.
- **Confusing activity with output.** Sending 50 messages with no replies and calling it a "good day."
- **Treating the channel as the problem.** When the script is the problem, or the offer is, or the niche is.
- **Diversifying too early.** 5 channels at 10% effort each will lose to 1 channel at 90% effort.
- **Inbound as procrastination.** "I'm building my brand" is what people say when they don't want to send 150 cold emails.
- **Outbound without inbound forever.** At some volume, manual outbound caps. If you don't start inbound by month 6, you will plateau.

## Sequencing for the first 12 months

**Months 1–3:** Outbound only. One channel. One stimulus, iterated. Goal: first 3–5 paying clients.

**Months 4–6:** Outbound at full volume + start inbound on one platform, 1–2 posts/week. Build the funnel. Goal: stable $10–20k/month.

**Months 7–9:** Outbound + inbound (now 2–3 posts/week, with case studies). Possibly hire a VA for outbound prospecting. Test paid ads small.

**Months 10–12:** Outbound becoming systematized/delegated. Inbound starting to produce a meaningful share of bookings. Paid ads scaling if profitable.

This is roughly the spine of the `ninety-day-plan-of-attack` and the year that follows.

## Diagnostic questions

1. What channel am I running, and how long have I run it consistently?
2. How many attempts have I made on the current stimulus before judging it?
3. Am I tracking reply rate, book rate, show rate, close rate?
4. Am I using inbound to avoid outbound?
5. Is my problem the channel — or the offer/niche/sales process upstream?

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Pick outbound first if you need cash this quarter.
- Pick one channel, run it at volume, for at least 30 days.
- Track all the numbers, all the time.
- Follow up 6+ times.
- Start inbound in parallel by month 4–6.

**Don't**
- Switch channels every two weeks.
- Skip outbound to do "brand building."
- Send 12 messages and conclude the channel is dead.
- Run 4 channels at 25% effort each.
- Layer paid ads onto a broken offer.

## Action items

1. Write down which one channel you'll run for the next 90 days. Commit publicly.
2. Build the daily activity target (see `appointments-fundamentals` math).

4. Block your inbound start date on the calendar — month 4. Don't start earlier.
