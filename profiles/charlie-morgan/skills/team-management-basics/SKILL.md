# Team Management Basics

> "How you behave on team meetings is how they will behave. If you're on your laptop clicking around while people talk, your whole team will be too. It's top down, always. You set the culture by being the person you want them to be." — Charlie Morgan, *6.2 Team Management Basics*

A small, deliberate set of tools and rituals is enough to manage a team of 1–10 contractors. You do not need an HR department, you do not need vision statements, and you do not need org-design consultants. You need: the right tools, contractors not employees, on-time payroll, monthly 1:1s, weekly team meetings, and the discipline to model the culture you want.

## When to use this skill

- "I have a VA. What's next?"

- The user has 1–5 people and feels lost on the management side.

## The honest scope of this skill

> "This doc is super short because at this stage of your business, you simply don't need to worry about building a team. I just want to tee you up so you have something to refer to when you start growing properly."

These are the *minimum* viable rituals. Once you're running real sales/setting/content departments, you'll need more — but for the 1–10 person stage this is sufficient. Don't over-engineer.

## The tool stack (Charlie's defaults)

- **Communication: Slack.** Not WhatsApp, not Teams, not iMessage. Slack.
- **Project management: Asana.** Notion or Monday.com are acceptable substitutes; Charlie uses Asana.
- **Files: Google Workspace / Google Drive.** Give scoped access; some folders stay locked to you.
- **Email: company.com email per hire** (~$10–$20/month/user via Workspace). No personal Gmail. Keeps it professional and revocable.

That's it. Four tools. Anything else at this stage is a distraction.

## Contractor, not employee

> "Hire people as exclusive contractors, not employees. This is a big one. Do not employ people."

The reason: employees come with legal obligations (pension, sick pay, mandatory holiday, can't be fired on a dime). Contractors are clean — terminable on contract terms, no payroll-tax overhead, no entitlement headaches.

**Caveats Charlie names:**
- Some jurisdictions don't allow long-term contractor relationships (misclassification rules). Talk to an accountant or lawyer for your country.
- For US/UK-based businesses hiring overseas (Philippines, Latin America), exclusive-contractor is generally the standard.
- The intent isn't to dodge protections workers deserve in a real employment relationship; it's that remote agency-style work genuinely fits the contractor model.

## Payroll discipline

- **Pick a fixed payroll day.** Charlie's company pays the 5th of every month for the prior month's work.
- **Never miss payroll. Ever.** Take it from your personal account, take a loan, sell something — but pay on time.
  > "If you haven't got the money, take it out of your own personal bank account or take a loan. Never miss payroll. That's how you die. The only reason people work for you is because they get paid."
- **Require invoices from contractors monthly.** Always verify them — match invoice line items to actual work delivered.
- This connects directly to `managing-finances-cashflow`: payroll is the line you don't cross.

## First hires beyond a VA

The typical sequence from solopreneur to small team:

1. **VA** (`virtual-assistant-masterclass`) — admin, ops, lead-gen support.
2. **Appointment setter** — once inbound/outbound volume justifies a dedicated booker.
3. **Sales rep / closer** — once you personally cannot take more sales calls.
4. **Delivery contractor / coach** — once your client load exceeds your hours.
5. **Ops / account manager** — once onboarding & client management become a full role.

Don't over-hire. Each role should replace a saturated category of *your* time.

## KPIs per role (the lightweight version)

You don't need a dashboard. You need one or two numbers per role.

- **VA:** SOPs completed on time / inbox zero / invoices reconciled.
- **Setter:** Calls booked per week / show rate.
- **Sales rep:** Close rate / cash collected / cycle length.
- **Delivery contractor:** Client outcomes hit / churn rate / NPS.
- **Ops:** Onboardings completed on time / SOP adherence.

Review these in the 1:1.

## Meeting cadence

### Monthly 1:1 with each direct report (15–30 min)

Purpose: feedback both directions. The four core questions:

1. "What's working in your role?"
2. "What's making your job harder than it needs to be?"
3. "What do *you* need from me to do your job better?"
4. "What's the thing you love most about your job, and the thing you hate most?"

Charlie's example of why 1:1s matter:
> "I did 1:1s with all my sales reps last month. Every single one of them mentioned a specific bad lead from a specific ad. I wouldn't have known if I hadn't asked. I went to marketing, killed the ad. That's what 1:1s are for — pulling the gripes out before they blow up."

The discipline: **don't snap at feedback.** If you bite their hand off the first time someone tells you something hard, they'll never tell you anything again, and you'll end up in a yes-man echo chamber.

### Weekly team meeting (45–60 min, fixed day/time)

Charlie runs Friday afternoons, 1 hour. Agenda:

1. Go around — each person says their **win for the week**.
2. Each person says **what they got up to** outside work.
3. Then: any items that need group discussion.
4. Then: it usually devolves into chatting. Let it. That *is* the culture.

Rules of engagement:
- **Don't interrupt.** Let people finish. You're the facilitator, not the orator.
- **Don't be autocratic.** "Walking up and giving a speech" is the wrong move.
- **Don't just talk business.** Ask about weekends, movies, life. People work remotely and isolated; this is the culture moment.
- **Don't be a friend.** Skirt the boundary — friendly but not buddies. The hammer needs to be implicit.

## Leadership posture (the top-down rule)

The thing Charlie repeats hardest:

> "How you behave is how they will behave. If you're on your laptop clicking around while someone talks, your whole team will be too. If you're on time, they're on time. If you bring focus, they bring focus."

Two stories from the transcript:

1. **The clicking manager** — a manager kept multitasking on team meetings. Within two weeks, his entire department did the same. Charlie called the team out on the meeting, then privately told the manager: "they follow your lead. Stop it." Behavior changed; problem solved.
2. **The smirking employee** — two people were DMing each other on Zoom and trying not to laugh. Charlie called them out by name in front of the whole team. After that, nobody played around in meetings.

The principle: **be willing to call things out the moment you see them.** Once. Cleanly. In front of others when warranted. The cost of *not* calling it out is a culture rot that's much harder to reverse later.

The complementary principle: **be the opposite charge of the room.** If energy is too low, lift it. If energy is over-the-top, ground it. Either extreme is a problem.

## Accountability mechanism

- Tasks live in Asana with owner + due date.
- Communication lives in Slack channels (one per project / one per team).
- Decisions and SOPs live in Google Drive.
- Wins and gripes surface in 1:1s and team meetings.

That's the loop. No fancy OKR system needed at this stage.

## Do / Don't

**Do:**
- Use Slack + Asana + Google Workspace + company emails. Period.
- Hire as exclusive contractors with explicit contracts.
- Pay on a fixed day every month, on time, no exceptions.
- Verify every invoice before paying.
- Run monthly 1:1s and a weekly team meeting.
- Model the culture you want — focus, presence, professionalism.
- Call out behavior the moment it appears.
- Let team meetings drift into casual chat. That's culture.

**Don't:**
- Don't hire as employees if you don't need to.
- Don't miss or delay payroll, ever.
- Don't be on your laptop during team meetings.
- Don't snap at feedback in 1:1s — you'll kill the channel.
- Don't try to be friends with your team. Skirt the line.
- Don't over-engineer the management infrastructure when you have 3 people.
- Don't autocratically lecture team meetings. Facilitate.

## Action items

1. Set up Slack workspace, Asana workspace, Google Workspace with custom domain emails for any current/future hires.
2. Pick a fixed payroll day. Put it in your calendar with a 3-day-before reminder.
3. Draft an exclusive-contractor agreement (have a lawyer review for your jurisdiction).
4. Schedule monthly 1:1s with each direct report. Save the four questions in a doc.
5. Pick a weekly team meeting slot (Charlie: Friday afternoon). Block it. Show up early.
6. Identify one behavior in your own meeting conduct that's modeling something bad. Fix it this week.
