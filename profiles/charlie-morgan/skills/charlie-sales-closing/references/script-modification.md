# Script Modification

> "The script we give you is very, very powerful out of the box, but I can promise you, it gets even stronger with a bit of tweaking." — Charlie Morgan, *4.4 Script Modification*

The Imperium script (see `imperium-conversion-playbook`) is a chassis. To make it fit your niche, offer, and voice, you modify some sections, leave others alone, and iterate based on real call feedback — not based on emotion. This skill is about *what* to modify, *what not* to touch, and the discipline of testing.

## When to use this skill

- "How do I adapt the script to my niche?"
- "Should I rewrite the discovery questions?"
- "How do I write my pitch / expertise statement?"
- "I sell SEO / e-com / coaching — does this still work?"
- "I want to change the script, what's safe to change?"
- "How often should I update my script?"
- The user is on script version 1.0 and considering version 2.0.

## The two rules of modification

### Rule 1: Understand the structure before you modify it

> "It would be like buying a car and then deciding that you want to personally modify the car, but if you don't really understand how the car works and the moving parts, then you're probably not gonna do a very good job."

If you don't yet understand *why* each section of the script exists, do not edit it. Go back to `imperium-conversion-playbook` and `sales-bedrock`. Modifying a section whose purpose you don't understand is how you break a working script.

### Rule 2: Change one variable at a time

> "If you test more than one variable and the outcome is different than it was before introducing the new variable, then it's impossible to deduce which variable caused the damage or the change."

Variables include: questions, tonality, pitch, rebuttals to specific objections, timing, silence, word choice, the order of two questions. Change one. Run 30 calls. Observe. Then change the next.

Charlie's plateau: he was stuck at 30% close rate for a year because he stopped iterating. A 30% plateau is just as painful as a 10% plateau — same psychology, same trap.

## What to modify vs what to leave alone

Walking through the script section by section:

### Section 1–2: Rapport + Agenda → leave alone

The agenda script is universal. Don't rewrite it. Just plug in your niche / outcome:

> "John, here's how I run these. I'm gonna ask you about [your problem area] for the first 15-20 minutes…"

### Section 3: Define current state (line of questioning) → modify lightly

Keep the core questions (where they are, how long, what they've tried). Add 1–2 questions specific to your niche.

- Gym owner → "What's lead quality been like from your current channel?"
- E-com → "What grinds your gears about your Facebook Ads right now?"
- SEO → "What positions are you currently ranking for the keywords you actually want?"

> "Don't remove any of these questions, just add one or two more that might be specific to your niche."

### Section 4: Indirectly cause pain → modify lightly

Same rule. Keep the universal pain-causing questions. Add niche-specific ones that dig into the *specific* failure modes your prospects experience.

### Section 5: Define their goal + future-pace → DO NOT TOUCH

> "Section six, define their goal and future-pace it. This is gonna be pretty universal. You won't really need to make any edits or changes here. Do not remove this question or change it. Please don't. It's the most important one in the whole script, really."

Even if it feels redundant, even if you think your prospects "know what they want already" — leave it. Removing it tanks close rates.

### Section 6: Money / can-they-afford-it → niche-dependent

For some niches (gyms in Charlie's case), if the prospect won't share revenue numbers you end the call. For others, you might be more flexible. Modify based on what your offer actually requires from a buyer.

### Section 7: Pitch / expertise statement → fully rewrite for your offer

This is the section you spend the most time on. The pitch follows a formula but the words are yours.

**Formula:**
```
I help [specific niche] achieve [specific outcome] by [sexy mechanism description]
```

**Niche** — be specific. Not "businesses." "Independent gym owners." "DTC e-com brands doing $30–100k/mo." "Solo coaches selling 1-on-1 high-ticket."

**Outcome** — concrete and quantified.

**Mechanism — make it sexy, not trite.**

> "Don't say something trite and boring like 'we do this by using SEO to rank for keywords.' No, you wanna say something sexier, like 'by manipulating Google's algorithm to get your site above all your competitors.' You wanna make it sexy. It should make your prospect lean forward."

> "We don't sell the Facebook ads. We sell the concept of it. Instead of saying 'The Gym Growth Accelerator Programme teaches you how to do Facebook ads,' we say 'The Gym Growth Accelerator Programme contains proven processes and marketing strategies designed for independent gyms and studios who want to scale their membership acquisition.'"

The buyer's ears should prick up. If the pitch sounds like every other agency they've heard, you've failed at this section.

### Section 8: Rebuttals → modify per niche / common objections

Take the universal rebuttals from `conquering-objections-vault` and translate them into niche-specific language. If you're selling to gym owners, the price objection answer references "members" and "monthly fees." If you're selling to e-com, it references "ROAS" and "MER." Same skeleton, different vocabulary.

## Pre-empting objections in the pitch

This is one of the most powerful modification moves. **If you know an objection is coming, neutralize it inside the pitch before they raise it.**

> "If you say what software you use and you say 'ClickFunnels,' they're going to object to it. So you've got to be clever and really just show the benefits of that and be so confident that when it comes round to them objecting, they can't, because they've already justified that it works. The battle is won before it is fought."

Examples:
- Prospect mentioned "leads from Facebook are crap" earlier in the call → in your pitch, explicitly address how *your* lead quality is different. Don't wait for the objection.
- Prospect has been burned by other agencies → in your pitch, name what's different about you and why this time will work.
- They're skeptical of the channel you use → frame the channel inside an outcome, not as a tactic.

> "The best way to win an argument is to avoid it." — Dale Carnegie, quoted by Charlie. Apply it: the best way to overcome an objection is to prevent it from arising.

## Voice and delivery — 80% of the work

> "Once you've written out your script, I want you to realize that writing the script is 20% of the battle. 80% of it is just figuring out how to deliver it in a way that is just potent."

What to practice on top of words:
- **Pauses.** After price, after the close, after a hard discovery question. Silence is pressure. Use it.
- **Tonality.** Where you go up vs down on a sentence. Drill the cadence.
- **Emphasis.** Which word in each line carries the weight.
- **Prolonged words.** Stretching one or two key words for emphasis ("we'll *guarantee* you results").

You don't refine these by reading the script. You refine them by recording yourself, listening back, and re-recording. Cringe is the price of competence (see `sales-bedrock`).

## The version cadence

> "The answers and the pitch and the expertise statement that you have in version 1.0 of your script will not be the same as version 2.0. The way that you progress from 1.0 to 2.0 is after you've done 30 calls."

Versioning rules:
- v1.0 → v2.0: after 30 calls.
- v2.0 → v3.0: after another 30 calls.
- v4 → v5 onwards: you can wait 100 calls between iterations as the script matures.
- **Never** modify mid-stream. The 30-call rule is non-negotiable. (See `oscillations-of-doubt`.)

If you change before 30 calls, you can't tell whether the change helped or hurt — and you'll spiral on emotion-driven edits forever.

## Charlie's tying-rebuttal-to-outcome trick

When a prospect raises a concern *during* discovery (not as an objection at the end), tie your answer back to the outcome they already told you they wanted.

> "I've basically just taken his question and the point that he's got about ClickFunnels and I've tied it in with his outcome. And then in doing that, I've tied our service to his outcome. And in doing that, I've added more confidence so that when he eventually asks about the price, he's got so much confidence that he's going to buy."

This works for price too. When they ask price:

> "Yeah, that's the best bit, I'm really excited to share this with you because we don't actually charge much. We should probably charge way more."

Pre-frame, then deliver.

## Do / Don't

**Do**
- Modify the pitch and expertise statement to your niche.
- Add 1–2 niche-specific discovery / pain questions per section.
- Rewrite rebuttals in the language of your buyer.
- Pre-empt known objections inside the pitch.
- Drill tonality, pauses, emphasis.
- Run 30 calls between versions.

**Don't**
- Don't remove the future-pace / goal definition question.
- Don't remove core discovery questions — only add.
- Don't rewrite the agenda script.
- Don't change multiple variables at once.
- Don't iterate before 30 calls of data.
- Don't use generic buzzwords ("Facebook ads", "SEO", "funnels"). Make it sexy.

## Action items

1. Read `imperium-conversion-playbook` and `sales-bedrock` first.
2. Write your pitch using the formula: *I help [niche] achieve [outcome] by [sexy mechanism]*. Test it on 3 people — do they lean forward?
3. Identify the top 3 objections specific to your niche and write rebuttals in that vocabulary (then load `conquering-objections-vault`).
4. Add 1–2 niche-specific questions to the discovery section. Don't remove anything.
5. List 2–3 pre-emptive lines you'll bake into your pitch to neutralize known objections.
6. Lock the script. Run 30 calls. Don't touch it.
7. After 30 calls: review recordings, identify ONE variable to change, ship v2.0.
