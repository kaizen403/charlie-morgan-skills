# Virtual Assistant Masterclass

> "A VA is your first hire. Not a salesperson, not a closer, not a delivery contractor. A VA. You're paying $5 an hour to buy your time back, and you're using that time to make $50, $100, $500 an hour selling and delivering. That's the arbitrage. That's the entire game at this stage." — Charlie Morgan, *6.1 Virtual Assistant Masterclass*

A virtual assistant is the first lever a solopreneur pulls to escape the time-for-money trap. Done right, a VA at ~$5/hr handles the admin sludge so you can focus on the high-leverage work. Done wrong (hiring too early, outsourcing the wrong things, no SOPs, no testing), it's a money pit and a distraction.

## When to actually hire (and when not to)

**Hire a VA when:**
- You have at least 1–3 paying clients and predictable cashflow to cover the VA's pay.
- Your calendar is full of low-leverage admin (invoicing, scheduling, inbox triage, support replies).
- You have at least one task you do repeatedly enough to write an SOP for.

**Do NOT hire a VA when:**
- You have no clients yet. You're not buying back time; you're inventing busywork.
- You're trying to outsource sales / delivery / content because you don't want to do them. (See "what not to outsource" below.)
- You don't have a single SOP written. You'll end up training in real time, badly, on someone else's clock.

## What to outsource and what NOT to outsource

**Outsource (yes):**
- Invoicing and payment chasing
- Inbox triage and basic email replies
- Calendar / scheduling management
- Customer support tickets (low-complexity)
- Data entry, CRM updates
- Research lists (lead-gen sourcing — but not the actual outreach)
- Document formatting, deck cleanup
- The "nitty-gritty admin" Charlie keeps referring to

**Do NOT outsource (these are yours):**
- **Sales calls.** You are the founder. The buyer wants to talk to you.
- **Appointment booking conversations / qualification.** Until much later.
- **Client delivery.** The thing you're being paid to do.
- **Content creation** in your voice. Your brand depends on it.

> "If you outsource the things that actually make you money — sales, delivery, content — before you've mastered them yourself, you're done. You don't know what good looks like, so you can't tell when it's bad. You'll burn clients and not know why."

## Where to find VAs (Charlie's named platforms)

- **OnlineJobs.ph** — primary recommendation. Filipino VAs, strong English, work ethic, professional remote culture, $5/hr typical rates.
- **Upwork** — broader pool but more variable quality and higher rates.
- Referrals from other operators in your niche are gold; ask in your communities.

Charlie's preference is the Philippines for the cost-to-quality ratio at the entry level.

## Pay rates Charlie cites

- **Entry-level VA:** ~$5/hr.
- The arbitrage example Charlie gives from his own life: he was apprenticing at $15/hr, hired a VA at $5/hr to do the admin, and netted $10/hr of his own time freed for the higher-leverage work.
- As VAs specialize (lead-gen, appointment setting, ops), rates rise to $7–$10+/hr.
- Always pay on time. **Never miss payroll.** (See `team-management-basics`.)

## Common VA roles

1. **General/admin VA** — invoicing, scheduling, inbox, support. The default first hire.
2. **Lead-gen VA** — sources lists, scrapes contact info, sets up Apollo / Instantly / outbound tools, runs initial outreach steps that don't require closing skills.
3. **Appointment-setter VA** — replies to inbound DMs / emails to book qualified prospects onto your calendar. Higher-skill, higher-rate. Hire only after you understand the booking process yourself.
4. **Ops VA** — handles client onboarding paperwork, integration checklists, follow-up cadences after delivery starts.

The progression: general VA → specialized VA(s) as you grow.

## How to train via SOPs

You can only outsource what you can describe step-by-step. The training method:

1. **Record yourself doing the task** (Loom). One screen capture per discrete task.
2. **Write the SOP** while watching the recording back: numbered steps, screenshots, decision rules ("if X, do Y; if Z, do W").
3. **Hand the VA the SOP first.** Don't explain it live. Let them try it.
4. **Audit the output.** Where did they get stuck? Update the SOP to handle that gap.
5. **Repeat.** The SOP is a living document.

Charlie's tell: a good VA *follows the SOP exactly*. A great VA tells you where the SOP is unclear.

## The hiring process

### Step 1: Job post
Be specific. State the role, the hours, the rate, English fluency requirement, and one quirky filter (e.g., "include the word 'parrot' in your reply") to weed out mass-applicants.

### Step 2: 10–15 basic interview questions
Async (written or short video). You're testing two things only:
- **Vibe** — are they pleasant, professional, present?
- **English fluency** — can they read instructions and reply clearly?

This is *not* a skill assessment. The skill comes from the SOP.

### Step 3: Narrow to 3–4 candidates
Based on vibe + fluency.

### Step 4: Paid test assignment via SOP
This is the real filter. Charlie's own example:

> "Take the 32-step scheduler-setup SOP from week 1 / week 5 of building outbound systems. Strip a couple of steps out. Hand it to the candidate and ask them to complete the task. The ones who follow the steps thoroughly *and* spot the missing pieces — those are the ones you hire."

You're testing:
- Can they follow detailed written instructions?
- Are they thorough? (Did they actually do all 30 steps, or did they skip 5 and lie?)
- Do they spot ambiguity and ask, instead of guessing?

Pay them for the test. ~1–2 hours of work at the VA rate. Cheap insurance against a bad hire.

### Step 5: Hire one. Probationary first month.
Make it explicit it's a 30-day probation. Run the management cadence below. If it's not working at day 30, end it cleanly.

## Management cadence

- **Daily:** quick async check-in (Slack / WhatsApp). What did you do yesterday, what are you doing today, any blockers?
- **Weekly:** 15–30 minute 1:1 video call. Review work, give feedback, surface friction. (See `team-management-basics`.)
- **Monthly:** longer 1:1 — role check-in, "what would make your job easier", payroll confirmation.

Pay on a fixed day every month. Never be late. Verify invoices.

## Firing

If a VA isn't working:
- **First**, audit your SOPs. Is the failure on their side or on your training?
- If SOPs are clear and they still aren't performing after one round of feedback, end it. Hire a contractor (not employee — see `team-management-basics`) so termination is clean.
- Be direct, professional, no drama. "This isn't working out. Here's your final pay. Thank you for the work."

## Scaling the team via VAs

- VA #1 (general admin) — frees ~10–15 hrs/week.
- VA #2 (lead-gen) — once outbound has a system to scale.
- VA #3 (ops/onboarding) — once you're signing 5+ clients/month and onboarding becomes the bottleneck.
- VA #4 (appointment setter) — when inbound volume justifies a dedicated booker.

Each new VA should be replacing a category of your time, not adding a vague "helper." If you can't write the SOP, you're not ready for that hire.

## Do / Don't

**Do:**
- Hire your first VA at OnlineJobs.ph for ~$5/hr once you have paying clients.
- Write SOPs *before* hiring, not after.
- Test candidates with a paid SOP-execution assignment.
- Run the daily/weekly/monthly cadence.
- Hire as exclusive contractors, not employees (see `team-management-basics`).
- Pay on time, every time.

**Don't:**
- Don't outsource sales, closing, delivery, or your voice/content.
- Don't hire a VA before you have client cashflow.
- Don't skip SOPs and "train as you go."
- Don't skip the paid test assignment — it's the single best filter.
- Don't be sentimental about firing. If the SOPs are clear and they can't follow them, end it.

## Action items

1. List every recurring admin task that ate your time last week.
2. Pick the top 3 highest-time, lowest-leverage ones. Write SOPs (Loom + Google Doc).
3. Post a job on OnlineJobs.ph with one filter phrase.
4. Run 10–15 interview questions on incoming applicants. Narrow to 3–4.
5. Send paid SOP test assignment. Hire the candidate who followed the steps and spotted gaps.
6. Set up Slack + Asana + Google Workspace email for the new hire (see `team-management-basics`).
7. Run daily check-ins for the first 30 days. Adjust SOPs as gaps emerge.
