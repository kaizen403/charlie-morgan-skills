# Managing Finances & Cashflow

> "Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. It's not about how much you make. It's about how much you keep. The way you treat thousands is the way you'll treat tens of thousands, hundreds of thousands, millions. Practice constraint now."

## When to use

Load this skill any time you (or someone you're coaching) is managing business money, deciding what to do with first revenue, designing a tracker, choosing software, separating personal and business accounts, planning for taxes, or feeling the pull to spend winnings on lifestyle.

This is the financial counterweight to every other Charlie Morgan skill. The whole curriculum exists to help you make money. This skill exists to help you keep it.

---

## Core Premise: Keeping Is Harder Than Making

The route to financial freedom is through constraint, sometimes for a decade or more. Most entrepreneurs never learn financial management because nobody ever taught them — not parents, not schools, not universities. So putting a business on top of a normal person reveals immediate financial incompetency.

Two truths:
1. **Most people who make money spend the same amount and end up with zero.**
2. **The Forbes Rich List has more self-made billionaires than inheritors. Why? Because keeping money is harder than making it. Inheritors don't have the muscle.**

You are at "step one" of building this muscle. Build it now, with the first thousand. The way you treat the first thousand is exactly the way you'll treat the first ten million. There is no later upgrade.

## The Financial Mindset Shift

> "Stop seeing your business as a tool to make you more money. Start seeing the money in your business account as a war chest you feed to make even more money. It is a feedback loop. You can go exponential. Buy the lifestyle when you can do it without noticing."

Old frame: business → money → spend.
New frame: business → money → war chest → reinvest → bigger business → bigger war chest → exponential compounding.

The yacht, watch, supercar, house — wait 10 years, ideally 15–20. By then the interest on your war chest covers the cost in a heartbeat. Spending early on visible lifestyle items is the single fastest way to keep yourself poor relative to your potential.

> "Poor people buy fancy shit so they can look richer than they are. Smart people reinvest money back into the business."

The "if I still want it in five years, I'll buy it" rule applies to every impulse purchase. Five years is nothing in your 20s and 30s.

### The salary discipline

Charlie pays himself a fixed monthly salary, just like a CEO would be paid by any company, even at 8 figures. The personal account stays small and predictable. The company account becomes a war chest that funds offices, key hires, acquisitions, ad budgets, and ultimately compounding growth. Everything personal — house, car, indulgences — comes out of the salary.

Apply this from your first $1,000. Pick a salary you can live on. Take that and only that. Leave everything else in the company.

## The First Tax Bill Trap

The first time you make real money in a year, your tax bill will hit you like a slap in the face if you have not been provisioning for it.

- UK: ~25% profit tax on company earnings (plus personal taxes on dividends/salary).
- US/EU/others: rates vary — find yours and forecast.

**Rule: every dollar that lands in the business, immediately separate the tax portion in your head (and ideally in a savings sub-account).** If your jurisdiction takes 25% on profit, then for every $100 of profit, $25 is not yours. Treat it that way from day one. Don't touch it.

If you spend money you should have set aside for tax, you'll spend the next several months working only to pay the government. Charlie has lived this. Every dollar of personal income for months went to a £40K back tax bill, while he had already adjusted to a lifestyle he could no longer afford. Avoidable. Don't repeat it.

## Financial Basics — The Setup

1. **Register your business officially.** (See `business-setup` skill / week 1 video 1.8.) Until then, you are mixing legal liability with personal life.

2. **Two separate bank accounts.** One personal, one business. Each with a checking + savings sub-account.
   - Personal checking: daily life.
   - Personal savings: emergency fund and longer-term reserves.
   - Business checking: ops.
   - Business savings: tax provision + war chest.

3. **Two separate cards.** One business debit/credit, one personal. Never mix.

4. **Avoid credit cards** unless you have already proved you can pay them off. Most people cannot. The "miles" / "points" arguments are not worth it. Charlie has *one* company credit card used to pay for ads (purely operational reason), and a small handful for the company at scale. Personally, debit only. Don't be incentivized to enter debt.

5. **Receipts: store every single business receipt** in a dedicated Google Drive folder. Take a picture *the moment you get the bill*. Charlie does this at client dinners, at restaurants, in the moment, drunk if necessary. If you don't capture it then, it is gone forever. The discipline is "before the next conversation continues, the receipt is in Drive."

6. **One card, one account, one merchant, one email.** Less is better. Eight-figure operations have multiple banks and cards out of necessity. You don't. Keep it brutally simple.

7. **Store every business email signup, subscription, software receipt** in the same place. Bookkeeping starts in the moment, not at year-end.

## The Daily / Weekly / Monthly Discipline

- **Daily:** Check your bank accounts. Look at your real position. Burying your head in the sand is the failure mode of most entrepreneurs.
- **Weekly:** Reconcile. Update the financial tracker. Note any unusual spending.
- **Monthly:** First weekend of the month, do the previous month's full reconciliation. Update line items, P&L, and forecast accuracy. Put it in your calendar — recurring event.
- **Quarterly:** Spend 1–2 days reviewing every line item. Cut everything you don't use. SaaS subscriptions, services, vendors. Most businesses bleed 10–30% of profit through forgotten subscriptions.

## The Financial Tracker (one Google Sheet)

The minimum viable financial tracker has four tabs:

### 1. Line items
A spreadsheet version of your bank statement, logged monthly:
- Date / Item / Amount / Category (income or expense type)
- Every single income line: "Payment from John Smith — Client 1 — £2,000"
- Every single expense line: "Office rent — £800", "Phone — £30", "Coffee for client — £3"
- Auto-sum at the bottom: total income, total expense, profit or loss for the month
- Repeat for every month, every year, indefinitely
- The pain of seeing expenses you regret will train you not to repeat them. That's the whole point of doing this manually instead of letting AI do it.

### 2. Cashflow forecasts
At the start of every month:
- List your offers: "Service 1 — £3,000", "Service 2 — £1,000"
- Forecast: how many sales you expect this month?
- At month end: how many sales did you actually make?
- Discrepancy column = forecast accuracy
- Same for expenses: forecast staff cost, ad spend, software, etc., compare to actuals

Why this matters: forecasting is a skill. Beginners are wildly wrong. After 12–24 months of doing this you become extremely accurate, which lets you spend less and make more deliberately.

### 3. Business expenses (recurring)
List every recurring monthly cost you know about:
- Software subscriptions
- Office rent
- Salaries
- Ad spend baseline
- Insurance
- Etc.

Auto-sum at the bottom = your monthly burn rate. You must know this number cold. You must always be able to answer "how many months of runway do I have?"

### 4. Personal expenses (recurring)
Same drill for personal: rent/mortgage, utilities, food, subscriptions, insurance, etc.
Sum = your personal burn rate = the floor of the salary you must take from the company.

## Accounting Software: QuickBooks

Use **QuickBooks** for proper bookkeeping. It integrates with banks, generates reports, runs payroll, and ties cleanly into whatever an accountant needs at year-end. Charlie uses it across his 8-figure org. You don't need a bookkeeper at startup scale — QuickBooks plus the manual tracker is enough until ~$50K/month or more.

QuickBooks has its own training library. Use it. You don't need a separate finance course at the start.

### Action item: 1 hour/day for 3–6 months on finance fundamentals

Read books. Watch videos. Learn double-entry, P&L, balance sheet, cashflow statement, taxes specific to your jurisdiction. There is no shortcut. If you intend to build a multi-seven or eight-figure company, financial literacy is a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have.

## When to Hire an Accountant

**Before your first tax filing**, hire a real accountant or accounting firm. They cost money. They save more money than they cost. The ROI is "biblical."

What a good accountant does:
- Files taxes correctly
- Identifies legal deductions you didn't know existed
- Advises on company structure (sole prop vs LLC vs LTD vs S-Corp etc.)
- Plans for tax events ahead of time (so April doesn't decimate you)
- Advises on banking structure, dividends, salary mix
- For international entrepreneurs: advises on incorporation jurisdiction, residency, structures

Charlie's recommendation in UK: TaxGem. (No kickback — just trust.) Find your local equivalent. Ask other founders. Interview at least three. Pick the one whose explanations actually make sense to you, not the cheapest.

## The Constraint Identity

This is the deeper move. The reason most lottery winners and first-year founders lose all their money is not bad math — it's identity. They identify as someone who "deserves" the lifestyle the money implies. They scale lifestyle to revenue. When revenue dips, they cannot scale lifestyle back down without psychological collapse.

The constraint identity says:
- I live on a fixed salary regardless of company performance.
- The war chest is sacred. It is not mine to spend personally.
- Lifestyle creep is an enemy, not a reward.
- I will buy the things I want when buying them is invisible — i.e., when the cost is a rounding error vs. my net worth.
- Until then, I optimize for compounding the war chest, not lifestyle.

Hold this for 5–10 years. You will out-compete every founder of your generation who didn't.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Mixing personal and business accounts.** You will get slaughtered at tax time and lose deductions you didn't know existed.
- **Spending the first windfall on lifestyle.** The first £3K client triggers an impulse to "treat yourself." Don't. That money is a deposit toward your war chest, not a lifestyle upgrade.
- **No tax provision.** Living on 100% of revenue and discovering at year-end you owe 25% to the government you don't have.
- **Not tracking receipts in the moment.** You will lose them. Always upload to Drive immediately.
- **Avoiding the bank account.** Looking once a month "because it makes me anxious." That is the failure pattern. Look daily. Honesty is the entire skill.
- **Hiring no accountant.** "I'll do it myself this year." You'll do it badly and either overpay tax or get audited. Hire someone.
- **No forecasting.** Living month-to-month with no projection. You will miss runway shortfalls and emergency expense spikes.
- **Letting expenses creep with no quarterly cleanup.** SaaS subscriptions, redundant tools, and vanity vendors compound silently. Quarterly review them all.
- **Buying credit-card-incentivized debt.** Don't.
- **Withdrawing the entire month's profit as personal income.** You will run out of war chest at exactly the moment you need it (a hire, a deal, a downturn).

## Action Items (do these now)

1. Open separate business and personal bank accounts (with savings sub-accounts).
2. Order separate debit cards for each.
3. Create a Google Drive folder titled "Receipts — [year]". Bookmark it on your phone.
4. Build the four-tab financial tracker spreadsheet.
5. Sign up for QuickBooks (or local equivalent) and connect your business bank.
6. Calculate your forecasted tax rate. Set up a savings sub-account inside your business and immediately move that percentage of every incoming payment.
7. Set a fixed monthly salary you transfer to yourself. Do not deviate.
8. Calendar: monthly bookkeeping reconciliation on the first weekend, quarterly subscription audit on the 1st of every quarter.
9. Schedule 1 hour/day for 3–6 months of finance learning.
10. Identify and contact at least three accountants. Pick one before your first tax year ends.
