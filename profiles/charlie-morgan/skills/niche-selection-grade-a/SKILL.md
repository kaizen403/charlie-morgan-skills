# Grade A Niche Selection

> "Step one, okay, is the niche." — Charlie Morgan, *1.4 Grade A Niche Selection*

The niche is the absolute foundation of the entire business. Pick the wrong niche and every downstream layer (problem, solution, offer, sales, acquisition) is broken. Most people pick badly because they pick from interest or from what's trendy. Charlie picks niches by criteria.

## When to use this skill

Trigger on any of these:
- "How do I pick a niche?"
- "Should I niche down?"
- "Is [X] a good niche?"
- "I'm thinking about pivoting to [Y]."
- "I can't decide between [niche A] and [niche B]."
- "My niche isn't working."

## Charlie's four niche criteria

A Grade A niche meets all four. Anything less is risky. Source: `week1/14_Grade_A_Niche_Selection.txt`.

### 1. Painful problem
The niche must have a problem that hurts. Not annoying — *painful*. The kind of problem that keeps the prospect up at night, costs them money daily, threatens their business, or blocks a goal they cannot let go of. If the problem is mild, no one pays premium prices to fix it.

> Test: Can you describe the problem in one sentence and does that sentence make a real business owner in this niche flinch?

### 2. Ability to pay
The niche must have money. Specifically, the prospects must already be paying for *something* in this category — agencies, software, consultants, ads, training. If they aren't paying anyone for anything related, the budget doesn't exist and you'll spend the next year evangelizing instead of selling.

> Test: Search for 3 competitors selling to this niche. Find pricing. If competitors charge $3K-$50K and have clients, the budget is there.

### 3. Easy to reach
You need to be able to find these people in concentrated places. Industry communities, professional associations, conferences, LinkedIn segments, niche subreddits, niche YouTube channels, niche Facebook groups. If they are scattered across the entire internet with no shared watering hole, your acquisition cost will be brutal.

> Test: Can you list 5 specific places (URLs, communities, conference names) where 100+ of these people gather?

### 4. Growing or large
The niche should either be expanding (rising demand, new entrants, regulatory tailwinds) or already large enough that you can serve it for years without saturation. Shrinking niches will eat you alive even if you do everything right.

## How Charlie picks

> "You need to find a group of people who have a painful, expensive, urgent problem that they're already trying to solve, and then position yourself as the person who solves that one specific problem better than anyone else."

His preferred process:

1. Brainstorm 10–20 candidate niches.
2. Score each against the 4 criteria (painful problem / ability to pay / easy to reach / growing).
3. Eliminate anything that fails on any criterion.
4. From the survivors, pick the one where **you have the highest information advantage** — prior experience, network, or genuine interest. (Information advantage compounds; pure interest doesn't.)
5. Commit. Don't change for at least 90 days of real testing.

## What "niching down" actually means

Most people think they're niched. They aren't.

- "I help businesses grow" — not a niche.
- "I help SaaS companies grow" — not a niche.
- "I help bootstrapped B2B SaaS companies between $10K and $100K MRR get to $250K MRR through cold outbound" — that's a niche.

Specificity is leverage. The narrower you are, the more you can charge, the easier it is to write copy, the easier it is to be *the* person for that problem.

## Common failure modes

- **Picking by interest only.** "I love yoga" doesn't mean yoga studios will pay you $5K/mo.
- **Picking too broad.** "Coaches" is not a niche. "Female health coaches doing >$3K/mo who want to scale to $15K/mo" is.
- **Switching every 3 weeks.** No niche works in 3 weeks. You're seeing variance, not signal. Hold for at least 90 days of real outreach (see `90-day-plan-of-attack`).
- **Picking a niche that fails the "ability to pay" test.** Broke prospects = broke business.
- **Refusing to validate.** Talk to 20 people in the niche before you commit. (See `niche-immersion-five-steps`.)

## Action items

After loading this skill, the agent should make the user do exactly one of:

1. **If picking:** brainstorm 10 candidates → score each on the 4 criteria → eliminate failures → name the survivor and commit.
2. **If second-guessing:** ask "have you done 90 days of consistent outreach in this niche yet?" If no, the answer is *stay*, not *switch*. The variance is too high to draw conclusions before then.
3. **If pivoting:** force the user to write down *why* the old niche failed against the 4 criteria. If they can't articulate the failure, they're chasing shiny object syndrome and the new niche won't fix anything.
