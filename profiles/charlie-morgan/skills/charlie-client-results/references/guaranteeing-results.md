# Guaranteeing Results

> "Money is not worth your integrity. If you take someone's money and you don't deliver, you give it back. End of conversation. There is no version of this where you keep the money and you sleep at night." — Charlie Morgan, *3.6 Guaranteeing Results*

A guarantee is risk reversal: you take the risk off the prospect's shoulders and put it on yours. Done right, it closes deals you'd otherwise lose, forces you to actually deliver, and keeps you on the right side of integrity. Done wrong, it bankrupts you or it's a marketing lie that makes you a scammer.

## When to use this skill

- "Should I offer a money-back guarantee?"
- "Prospects keep saying 'how do I know this will work?'"
- "What kind of guarantee is safe for me to offer?"
- "I sell mindset coaching / spiritual work / a vague intangible — why can't I close?"
- "A client is asking for a refund. Do I give it?"
- "How do I write the guarantee into the contract?"
- The user is brand-new and considering guaranteeing results before they've validated the methodology — they need the warning in this skill before they make that promise.

## The ethical mandate (read this first)

> "If you take someone's money and you can't get them the result you promised, you refund them. That is not a strategic decision. That is a moral one."

Charlie's non-negotiable: the moment it's clear the client isn't going to get the outcome, you give the money back. Not because of the contract. Because keeping money you didn't earn is theft, and the entire premise of high-ticket services collapses if you let yourself off that hook.

This is also why **brand-new operators should not pour client money into operating expenses, ads, or lifestyle.** If you have to refund and you've already spent it, you're now in a hole. Hold client cash in reserve until delivery is locked in.

## The warning: don't guarantee what you can't deliver

A guarantee is only as honest as the methodology behind it. New entrants who haven't validated their delivery (haven't yet hit the sample sizes from `iterative-expertise`) are guaranteeing on hope. That's how scammers are made — not always intentionally, but functionally.

Rule: **don't offer a guarantee until you have evidence your methodology actually works.** Until then, sell the work, not the outcome.

## The three types of guarantee

### 1. Performance guarantee (results-based)

"You'll hit X result in Y time, or I refund / keep working free."

- **Strongest closer.** Removes nearly all prospect risk.
- **Highest danger to you.** Requires a methodology you've actually validated.
- Must be tied to a *measurable* outcome. "More leads" is not measurable. "30 booked appointments in 60 days" is.
- Must include client-side responsibilities. If they don't show up, the guarantee voids.

### 2. Satisfaction guarantee (subjective)

"If you're not happy in the first 30 days, full refund."

- Easier to offer. No specific outcome promised.
- More open to abuse — "happy" is undefined.
- Useful early on when you don't yet have data to make performance promises.
- Pair it with a hard window (14 / 30 days) so it can't be invoked indefinitely.

### 3. Conditional guarantee (the workhorse)

"If you do X, Y, and Z on your end, and we don't hit [specific outcome] by [date], we keep working until you do / you get a full refund."

- This is the structure Charlie steers people toward.
- It's a performance guarantee with explicit client obligations.
- Closes hard because it's specific. Protects you because the conditions are real.

## How to structure a guarantee so you don't get burned

1. **Make the outcome specific and measurable.** "30 qualified appointments in 60 days," not "more leads."
2. **Define the client's obligations in writing.** Show up to weekly calls. Approve creative within 48 hours. Provide testimonials by day 7. Anything they have to do for you to deliver — list it.
3. **Specify the remedy.** Refund? Continue working free? Partial refund pro-rata? Pick one and write it down.
4. **Time-box it.** "Within 60 days of onboarding completion." No open-ended guarantees.
5. **Put it in the contract.** See `client-onboarding-contracts`. If it's verbal, it doesn't exist.
6. **Reserve the cash.** Don't spend the client's payment until you're past the guarantee window or delivery is locked in.

## The intangible offering trap

Charlie's specific critique of mindset coaches, spiritual coaches, "third-eye openers," and burnout consultants:

> "If you can't make your outcome tangible, your outreach won't work, your sales calls won't work, your ads won't work. 'Open your third eye' is not a result. 'Reduce your burnout' is not a result. You have to make it measurable, even if your work is intangible."

The fix: translate the intangible work into a measurable proxy.

- "Reduce burnout" → "Reduce cortisol levels by 50% measured by saliva test in 90 days."
- "Open your third eye" → not salvageable as a high-ticket offer; pick a different niche.
- "Improve mindset" → "Increase weekly billable hours by 40% / lose 10 pounds / hit revenue target X."

If you can't define a measurable proxy, you don't have an offer; you have a vibe. Vibes don't get guaranteed and they don't sell at high ticket.

## Charlie's exact language on the call

When closing with a guarantee:

> "Look, here's how this works. If you do X, Y, and Z on your end, and we don't get you to [specific outcome] in [timeframe], you don't pay. Or you get a full refund. Or we keep working for free until we do. The risk isn't on you. It's on me."

When refunding:

> "I told you I'd get you the result. We didn't get there. Here's your money back. I don't want to keep money I didn't earn."

Direct. Unembellished. No defensiveness, no negotiation, no salvage attempts. The moment you start arguing the refund, you've lost the integrity the guarantee was supposed to protect.

## Do / Don't

**Do:**
- Offer a guarantee once your methodology is validated (3+ successful clients minimum).
- Make the outcome measurable.
- Spell out client obligations.
- Hold client cash in reserve until you're past the guarantee window.
- Refund without drama when you fail. It protects your name far more than the cash protects your bank account.

**Don't:**
- Don't guarantee on day one with no track record.
- Don't promise vague outcomes ("more growth," "better mindset").
- Don't spend client money before you've delivered.
- Don't argue, slow-roll, or ghost when a refund is owed.
- Don't write the guarantee in marketing copy and forget to put it in the contract.
- Don't offer open-ended guarantees with no time limit.

## Action items

1. Audit your current outcome promise. Is it measurable? If no, rewrite it until it is.
2. Decide which of the three guarantee types fits your stage. Default to conditional once validated; default to satisfaction-only when new.
3. Write the guarantee, the client obligations, the remedy, and the time window into your contract template (see `client-onboarding-contracts`).
4. Set up a separate holding account or sub-balance: client payments stay there until past the guarantee window. See `managing-finances-cashflow`.
5. Pre-write your refund script so you're not improvising under emotional pressure.
