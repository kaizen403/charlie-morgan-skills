# Mapping Your Methodology — Bringing Order to Chaos

> "Right now, you've got this chaos — information overload, all this information flying around your brain, hundreds of different points, dozens of influences. Through this video, we're trying to string out this chaos into order, then turn it into a staircase your clients can climb."
> — Charlie Morgan

## When to use this skill

This is **step 3 of the six-step synthesiser process** (after `defining-problem` and `critical-information-overload`). Use this skill when you have a Google Drive full of notes, a brain full of competitor copy, and now must produce **a single coherent linear sequence of actions** that solves the client's problem.

Triggers:
- "I've consumed everything; now what?"
- Drafting your service deliverable / SOP
- Distilling a methodology from competitor research
- Filtering which claims to trust
- Sales scripts ask for your "process" and you don't have one
- Onboarding new staff on what your service actually is

## The first principles: a methodology must be A.L.C.O.

A working methodology obeys **four unbreakable rules**. Miss one and it fails.

### 1. Action-based
A methodology is *only* a string of actions. Not theory, not concepts, not a vibe. Someone — you (DFY), you-and-them (DWY), or them (DIY) — has to execute it. Every piece of theory in the methodology must give ascent to action.

### 2. Linear
Steps occur in **sequence**. Step 2 cannot be done before step 1. Clients shouldn't be able to get the result without going through every step.
> "Action 1 → Action 2 → Action 3. If you don't get this, you're in deep trouble."

### 3. Critical
Each step must move the needle. Pareto applies — most actions in a typical service add ~5%; you want only the asymmetric high-leverage actions in v1.
> "Less is more. I cannot stress that enough. Less is more."

Charlie's example: posting to a gym's Facebook page slightly helped retention. Compared to ads, DBR (database reactivation), and funnels, it was negligible — so it didn't make the methodology.

### 4. Objective
Every action must be informed by **information that is true**, not popular, not confidently delivered, not from authority alone. Faulty information dressed as critical will sink your methodology and your reputation.

The methodology is good if it is also **fast, easy, simple**:
- Fast: solves the problem as quickly as possible
- Easy: client/you can execute without misery
- Simple: communicable in plain language

## The Minimum Viable Methodology (MVM)

> "What we want to avoid doing is overcooking your methodology. People build this whole, really complicated thing with all these moving parts. You want something as simple, lean, straightforward, and fast as possible."

Why MVM not "perfect":
- You're going to be wrong on v1 anyway
- Iteration is the only true mechanism of improvement (`iterative-expertise`)
- Few moving parts = easy to isolate what's broken
- More moving parts = more failure modes and more onboarding friction

Aim: roughly **5–10 actions in sequence** for your v1. If you have 30, you've overcooked.

## How to discern criticality and objectivity — the seven-step critical thinking process

Apply this to every "fact" you might bake into your methodology.

### Step 1 — Gather and define the information
Take a single discrete claim from your overload. State precisely what it is asserting, beneath your own biases.

### Step 2 — Understand the objects (MECE)
Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. Break the statement into its component objects:
1. **Remove yourself.** Third-party observer view.
2. **Remove identities.** Strip emotionally-charged collective nouns.
3. **Identify the objects.** Nouns the claim is about.
4. **Identify the relationships** between objects. Detective pinboard.
5. **Identify the time sequence.** What happened in what order.
6. **Remove ambiguity.** One word per object — "shirt" or "T-shirt", not both.
7. **Remove emotion.** Briefly. Just to see clearly.
8. **Remove loops.** A claim repeated 5x is no truer than once.

### Step 3 — Examine the argument
Every piece of information makes a *claim* (what it asserts true) supported by a *premise* (why) that yields a *conclusion*.
- **Find the claim.** What is it asserting?
- **Find the premise.** What is the reason / evidence?
- **Judge the argument.** Strong premise → stronger claim. "Trust me bro" vs "10 articles + 5 RCTs + tracked data" — order of magnitude difference.
- **View from all angles.** Take the perspective of the doctor, the patient, the patient's mother, the patient's insurer.
- **Examine the motive.** What does the source gain from you believing it? (Russell Brunson dunking on Infusionsoft. Doctors paid by tobacco. Always **follow the money.**)

### Step 4 — Remove biases
- **Remove authority.** If anything, distrust authority more. (Doctors said smoking was healthy.)
- **Remove consensus.** Popular ≠ true. (Food pyramid.)
- **Remove popularity of the speaker.** Charisma is not evidence.
- **Remove your priors.** What would change your mind?

### Step 5+ — Test against reality
- Does this match what real operators do?
- Did multiple independent sources arrive at it without copying each other?
- Does it survive a Socratic "why" chain?
- Can you explain the cause-and-effect mechanism?

## The methodology creation pathway as a system

```
Input  : All gathered information (overload + interviews + competitor copy)
Process: Your brain critically thinking + first-principles filtering
Output : Linear MVM (v1)
Feedback: Sign clients → observe → iterate (see iterative-expertise)
```

## Step-by-step: build your MVM

1. **List every distinct action** suggested by your research. Brain dump. ~50–200 items.
2. **Cluster** duplicates and near-duplicates. Reduce to 20–30 distinct actions.
3. **Apply A.L.C.O.** Strike anything that fails action / linear / critical / objective. Should be 8–15 left.
4. **Rank by leverage.** Top decile of impact wins. 5–10 actions.
5. **Sequence them linearly.** What logically must come first? What unlocks the next?
6. **Define the "done" state** for each action. What artefact / outcome confirms completion?
7. **Identify constants** (things you won't iterate on yet) and **variables** (things you might A/B later).
8. **Write it as a numbered staircase.** Not paragraphs — numbered steps.
9. **Pressure-test each step against:** Is it necessary? Is it ordered correctly? Is it true? Is it actionable?
10. **Stop.** Do not polish further. v1 ships. The rest is iteration (load `iterative-expertise`).

## Do / Don't

**DO**
- Aim for 5–10 actions, not 30
- Write the methodology as a numbered list
- Strike-through anything that doesn't survive A.L.C.O.
- Trace every claim's premise before you build on it
- Test for incentives behind every "expert fact"
- Keep notes of what you removed and *why* — useful when iterating

**DON'T**
- Polish v1 to perfection
- Trust authority figures unconditionally
- Confuse popularity with truth
- Add "nice to haves" that don't move the needle
- Skip the critical-thinking pass on individual claims
- Write paragraphs instead of steps

## Common failure modes

- **Overcooked v1.** 25-step methodology no client will follow.
- **Smuggling in faulty info** because the speaker was charismatic.
- **Non-linear methodology** — clients can skip steps, results are inconsistent.
- **Theory disguised as action.** "Adopt a growth mindset" is not an action.
- **No defined "done" per step.** Can't tell if it was executed.
- **Authority worship.** "X said it, so it's in my method."
- **Single-source dependency.** Whole methodology copied from one guru — when their model breaks, yours does too. Triangulate.

## Action items

1. Brain dump every distinct action your research surfaced. Don't filter yet.
2. Apply A.L.C.O. to each item. Strike anything that fails.
3. Reduce to a 5–10 step sequence.
4. For each step, write a one-sentence "done state."
5. For your top three "facts" baked into the method, run the seven-step critical thinking process. Verify premise. Check incentive of source.
6. Document v1 in a single Google Doc. Title it "MVM v1." Date it.
7. Move to `iterative-expertise` immediately — do not over-polish.
